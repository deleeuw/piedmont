Introduction

Writing a chapter about Lewis Love is somewhat daunting. He had a long, rich, and active life.

If I had enough time and energy left I could probably write a 250-page book about Lewis Love and his immediate family, but I have many more Piedmont history chapters to write.  I am just mentioning the potential Lewis Love book to show how much happened during his life, and how much material there still is.

Even for my more limited project there is much more documentation I have to go through than for Ulery, Fenstermacher, Smith, and Howe combined. Love had many children, who largely remained in the Portland area, and generated many newspaper items of their own. He had much more land in his donation land claim than the others, because he was married and arrived in Oregon early. He also lived longer, and he was involved in multiple minor and major lawsuits, which also generated a lot of coverage. So this promises to be a rather long chapter, with much biographical detail.

There will be separate sections on the members of his immediate family, his wife and children.

Because this book concentrates on real property transactions, we only discuss the children, because after the death of Lewis Love they almost immediately got rid of his land. The grandchildren were no longer involved in selling tracts and subdividing neighborhoods. Unfortunately, but not surprisingly, not much is known about his wife and the daughters. Their assigned role in life was to marry young, get a lot of children, and “keep house”. There are some minor exceptions, but generally it’s patriarchy throughout.

One more introductory remark. The modern Piedmont neighborhood tends to be identified with the original Piedmont subdivision of 1888. For example, the motto of the Piedmont Neighborhood Association is taken from the brochures of the Investment Company, the logo is either the bandstand in Peninsula Park, right next to original Piedmont, or the water tower that the Investment Company built for the Piedmont subdivision. But the part of the Piedmont subdivision that is in the Piedmont neighborhood is only about ten to fifteen percent of the neighborhood area, while the Lewis Love DLC is at least half of the Piedmont neighborhood. A pretty good case could be made for changing the name of the Piedmont neighborhood to the Love neighborhood, or simply to Lewis Love.

Of course it is true that the southern part of the neighborhood, below Bryant, is among the oldest parts and was certainly initially the most affluent. But one of the main reasons for this is that Lewis Love did not sell or develop any of his acreage, and that development north of Bryant only started more than twenty years after Piedmont was platted and developed. In those twenty years North Portland built the Swift meat packing plant, and many other industries, and the people working in those plants needed housing. That created demand for a different type of housing than the homes for comparatively well-off people fleeing the rowdy and immoral West Side.

To some extent this chapter tries to remedy an unfortunate situation. The Sunday Oregonian of  January 7, 2008 had a nice article about Lewis Love in John Terry’s Oregon’s Trails series with the title: Pioneer’s name all but forgotten in public domain. Here it is:

Although the piece is useful, it has several errors. As we have seen, the southern boundary of Love’s property in North/Northeast Portland was not Lombard but Bryant. Although there may not be a great deal of Love memorabilia left standing downtown, the names of the Piedmont subdivisions certainly have a lot of Love in them. I guess John Terry, together with the rest of the Oregonian, tends to pay more attention to the other side of the Willamette. This also explains why Lewis Love was “all but forgotten”.

Portland history, like most local history, tends to concentrate on the people who started living in the town center, were active in public life, flaunted their riches, donated stuff, and advertised their name. Lewis Love amassed his fortune in a rather unspectacular and fairly modest way. Although he was active on the west side, and actually lived on First Street and Front Street for a long time, his most important accomplishments were in Washington Territory, on the Columbia River, and in North and Northeast Portland. And events in those areas were inherently less visible than those on the west side. His children stayed on the east side and platted and developed his huge land holdings there. Although it is true that Lewis Love does not feature prominently in Portland history books such as the ones by McCall, Abbott, and Lansing, his name does live on in the names of the subdivisions Loveleigh, Lovewood, Love’s Addition, and the Green C. Love Addition.

I hope this chapter shows how Captain Love’s trajectory, starting poor and becoming rich, was ultimately based on huge swatches of stolen lands given away by the US government.  The purpose of this generosity was to attract settlers from the East to replace Native Americans and to finalize discovery and manifest destiny claims. Within this framework, which is of course basically corrupt, Lewis Love was a remarkable man. He saw and took opportunities that were offered to white people at the time, creating and abandoning businesses when appropriate, never being discouraged by disaster, using and misusing the law, and having a truly incredible amount of physical and mental power and energy. And, throughout, he saw and emphasized the value of land. Holding on for more than 50 years to his 700 acres in North and Northeast Portland, and not selling any of it during his lifetime, was definitely a prudent decision. And above all it was very family-friendly, because ultimately it greatly benefited all his children and grandchildren.

Life

In November 1899 Captain Lewis Love sat down with his granddaughter Harriet LaVelle Peacher and dictated a manuscript with the details of his trek across the plains from Missouri to Oregon, and about his subsequent activities and enterprises in Oregon and Washington. There are nine nicely cleaned up and retyped pages. A second seventeen-page manuscript, by Captain Love’s great-granddaughter Clara Love Stone goes through Captain Love’s manuscript and provides many additional details and references. These manuscripts are the holy grail of Lewis Love scholarship. I will refer to them in this chapter as the Love Manuscript and the Love Commentary, with a capital M and a capital C. They are reproduced here in total, because we will use them as the starting point for much of the chapter. As you will see, a great deal of attention is paid to Love’s travels on the Oregon Trail, which probably was the most exciting period of his life, although surely not the most profitable one. In the text we have incorporated the underlined and numbered references to the Love Commentary by Ms. Stone, which follows the Love Manuscript.

PREFACE

Captain Love was born in New York, Chautauqua Co., June 12, 1818. After

living there till he was 15 years of age he moved to Fox River Co., Illinois

in the year 1833. From there he moved to Missouri in 1845 and crossed the

plains to come West in 1849. His mother was part Pennsylvania Dutch and part

Yankee; she was born in New York and her name before she was married was Jane

Demont (deMott). She was the mother of 6 boys and 2 girls all of which are

dead (Nov. 1899) except Captain Lewis Love.

Mrs. Love, Captain Lewis Love's wife was born in Tennessee, near a little

town called Spy and moved to Illinois in 1833 where she met Mr. Love and they

were married in 1836. Mrs. Love's Mother's name was Edwards before she was

married and Mrs. Love's name was Griffith. Her Mother was from Tennessee. She

was born before the Revolution and has been dead 70 years.

There was born to Captain Lewis Love and wife six boys and 2 girls, namely

Mary C. Love, Melinda Love, Green Love, Lewis Love, Fred Love, William Love,

Jeremiah and Joseph beside four children which they raised but never adopted.

There was three boys drowned, namely William Love and --- and --- (Names

not given in the manuscript for the latter two)

CIRCUMSTANCES CROSSING PLAINS

The train which Captain Lewis Love crossed the plains in started from

Lawrence Co., Mo. (1), and when they got thirty miles north of Ft. Scott,

they came to high water called the Osage River.

There was only three families together at that time, so they went to

work putting in timbers to make a raft to cross on about 2:00 P.M. Two

Cherokee Indians rode up and asked us what we were doing. They were on

their way to the gold fields in California. We told them we were putting

in timbers to build a raft to cross the high water, so they said for us to

turn our teams out and their train would come up that evening with 35

wagons and 85 men. So we waited till next morning, then went to work

building the raft.

We got it all together in good shape in a stream called Sugar Creek (2), this

being backwater from the Osage River. We floated down to the Osage so as

to be able to cross both streams at once. There we found the river to be

very swift, too much so to hold against the current. Then we wondered how

we was going to cross. Finally Capt, Love suggested that we take grapevines (3)

and make ropes. Two or three of the Indians kept interfering till the main

body drove the others off and told them to stay until it was time for them

to cross. Capt. Love took one man with him and they pounded the vines till

they could be beat to make a cable. They made a cable to reach a quarter

of a mile and forked it down near the raft to keep it from swinging around

so as to pull across straight. We then swam over and took ropes to pull

us in the direction we wanted to go. We put one wagon on the raft at a

time and each trip the raft would sink knee deep till we struck the swift-

est part of the river, then the raft would rise on top and float from there

over. We commenced taking things across at 2:00 P.M. and that night at

10:00 P.M. everything was over safe. From that on the Cherokees became so

much attached to me they would ride three or four miles to see me.

Soon afterwards Cholera set in and it was not uncommon for four or five

cases to be reported in camp, the Cherokees being about the heaviest suf-

ferers according to the number, and they died off very fast.

The Ninth Regiment that crossed the plains that year took them in tow and

kept them for several hundred miles until they were able to take care of

the teams themselves, then they were discharged. And the last I saw of

them was on South Pass in the Rocky Mts. They rode past us four miles and

drove back to bid me farewell.

Not long after we left the Osage River we came to a bluff where we had to

let the wagons down by means of trees and ropes. Some of the trees had

gashes cut in them fully two inches from letting down so many wagons. We

had not traveled far when an axle broke in one of the wagons so we went

along up the canyon a ways till we came to some ash trees. We hewed a

piece of ash down and made another axle.

One morning while we were camping at a place called Potowatammy, I went

out to hunt up the cattle. When I found them there were 41 missing. The

word came that Indians were seen driving them away up the road, The man

that was with me wanted to go that way to find them. I told him, No, it

was a hook (hoax) to keep us from finding them. I told him I was too well

acquainted with the Indians to believe they would steal my cattle. So we

struck across the plains about four miles to a high ridge which had quite

a stream of water at the foot of it. There I saw three squaws riding across

the plains. I hailed them, and as I could talk a little of their language,

I asked them if they had seen any cattle. So they motioned up over the

ridge where there was three men herding them and just ready to start off

with them. I wanted to know of them what they were doing with those cattle,

and they said they was sent back by a train about four miles ahead to gather

them up. I told them it was no such a thing for my cattle were all branded

and there was the brand to show for itself. They had 13 head of oxen of

mine which would have left me only one to work. When we got to Ft. Carney

(Kearney) I took a man by the name of Miller in the train to assist. The

rule was that two men was to stand guard every night and when it came my

turn he never would let me go but he would take my place. When we got

pretty well to Ft. Laramie there was five men and a wagon came into the

train. One of the men was a great big stout fellow and to let him tell

it he could whip all the Indians on the plains. They took a  notion one

day, quite a number, that he was a coward and along towards night he was

pointing out how many pony tracks there was. So they thought they would

play a joke on him. It was his night to be on duty, and of course if any

Indians came around they knew he would shoot them. One old man in their

wagon called "Pap" on account of his age told him he had been an old front-

iersman and would like to load his gun for him. So it was loaded with a

paper wad, After supper the two men went out on guard. The second man had

been informed of the joke so he was all ready for them. A while later five

men went out, three of whom had guns, and went around in behind the men and

two of them commenced patting on their knees to draw his attention. His

bravery left him very suddenly and, making the best speed possible for the

camp, told them the Indians were after him and he had come for help. Next

morning quite a crowd gathered around in one wagon, and were talking about

the Indians' attack and one person asked him how many Indians he really

thought there was. He said there was 25 or 30 in front and they occupied a

piece of ground the shape of a flatiron, and back of this number their heads

stuck up as thick as they could be.

After we crossed South Platte, apast Ft. Laramie, two or three days' ride,

we stopped to noon one day. While we were eating, two men came back a-

horseback. They were in haste and wanted us to drive on as they wanted help.

The time came for the train to start and several more trains drove up and a

German man belonging to one of the trains was very much excited over the re-

port of the Indians and went on to tell how they would kill us. I went up

to him and put my hand on his shoulder and told him he must not talk that way

because he was frightening the women and children. He says, "You are right",

and you could hardly hear another word out of him after that. When we got

straightened around ready to start, we traveled about two miles, when we

found we had to turn around and come back about five miles. We started down

Ash Canyon. After going down some distance the same two men came riding

back further up on the offside of the teams, yelling for the women and

children to get into the wagons, that the Indians were advancing. The men

reported 1500 to 2000 Indians. They came on around where I was and I stepped

out and told them the same as I told the German man, and if they didn't stop

I would knock them off their horses. As soon as they passed on, I took my

gun and went to see what there was over the hill. ‘Iwo women went with me.

We went to the top of the hill. There we stopped and estimated about 75 or

80 Indians out of the 1500 or 2000. ‘Then we went back down the hill and met

our teams. We then reported the number of Indians. A great many would not

believe us on account of the large number being reported before. We then

drove on out to the mouth of the canyon, having about 80 horsemen in front,

well-armed. When we got down to where the Indians were we rode on down

amongst them and found everything very peaceful after all the scare. It

was a remnant of the Sioux tribe who had just been engaged in a battle with

a tribe called the Blackfeet. I wanted to know of those fellows after the

trains had stopped, why they had made so much fuss about the Indians. They

said one Indian made a mark across the road in the dust. Those were the

first Indians we had seen since leaving the Potowatammy tribe, so many

Indians had left the trail on account of the Whites and we only saw a few

buffalo.

Close to Independence Rock, which was literally covered with names, quite a

number of people had gathered together. One train of 52 wagons and car-

riages drove up which took passengers and freight. That train held a meet~

ing and decided to throw out 1,000 pounds of freight, some offering a dol-

lar a pound to get it hauled through. They went to work unloading the freight.

Amongst other things there was quite a large quantity of powder. They took

it and poured it in a circle around quite a large piece of ground out of the

kegs; then put a fuse to it and burnt the circle in that way, and a great

many articles that was supposed to have been thrown out were shipped in other

Wagons.

After that we came to a great many places where wagons and buggies were left

standing different places on the plains. One instance I unhitched from a

2-horse wagon and left it standing with the cover on. I asked the people if

they objected to my leaving mine and taking the other one. They said no, I

was welcome to it, so I changed the load and left my wagon standing with the

cover on.

It was a very common thing for the oxen to get sore feet. So we would take

a piece of skin from a dead ox and sew it around the sore feet to keep the

sand from getting into them. Nothing more of much importance occurred till

we struck the Black Hills. Two men had gone ahead to find a camping place

at the foot of the Black Hills, and when they got there they reported no

grass to be found -- nothing but sagebrush. They said we would have to tie

our cattle up with nothing to eat. I got on my mule and asked if any man

would go with me to hunt grass. It was in the P.M. We struck out for a

high rocky ridge. When we got about 2/3 of the way to it, the Irishman

would not go any further. He said there was no use. I went about a mile

and then had to get off my muie and walk, it was so rocky. I went on over

the ridge and down to the creek bottom and found some grass and brush, etc.

I went back and took the cattle over, which gave them 2/3 of a feed. A

watchman stayed on the hill overnight. Next morning I went over with the

rest to drive the cattle back and then I told them about there being quite

a number of Indians camping along the creek. They thought I hadn't been

down there, but after a while they found the camps as I had told then.

Then we drove on a half day and stopped to noon and a man by the name of

Dotworthy and myself went down on the ridges to see if we could find any-

thing to shoot. There we picked up a chunk which afterwards proved to be

gold. We looked at.it awhile and then threw it down and left it. No doubt

the piece was worth $1000.00.

When we reached Bare River Hills, there we saw the first snow and straw-

berry blossoms stick up to the edge of the snow. When we got down to Snake

River, we found plenty of Indians. I used to practice lifting with them.

I had one wagon that was pretty heavily loaded. I would turn my back to

the wheel and take hold of the spokes and then motion for some of the big

Indians to come and lift it. But I never found but one that could lift it.

Then the Indians would point to me and say I was so much stronger than they

were. When we came to Salmon Falls the Indians came to us every day and

wanted to swap salmon for shirts. It didn't make any difference how ragged

they were just so they could get them. Sometimes they would get a little

sulky because we wouldn't trade with them. We traveled all the way down on

the South side of Snake River and the first country we found that appeared

to be muddy was along Powder River. When we started over Grand Round Valley,

I thought it the most beautiful country we had found yet. There I saw the

largest bunch of Indian ponies I had ever seen. There were fully 2000 in

the number.

When we started up into the Blue Mountains there for the first time we came

into tall timber, and crossing Grand Round (Ronde) River you could see salmon

darting in every direction. After crossing the first mountain we found some

very rich land. When we crossed the first mountain before we crossed the

river there was about a dozen Indians traveled with us for two weeks. I had

a spring wagon with me, which the Indians had never seen before. Every night

they would deposit a package of some kind in that wagon and would remove it

again every morning, and would talk about the wagon and appeared to be very

much pleased. They would camp at night and build a fire, and lay two tent

poles parallel on the ground; then they would form on each side and sit down

with sticks in their hands and commence singing. The first Indian would have

a stick sharpened to a point at each end, just long enough so he could hold

it in his hands without it being seen. Then he would throw it from one hand

to the other and the one that guessed which hand the stick was in got the

stick and repeated the same thing. This was their way of gambling. They

seemed very much delighted to think they could travel along with us. We

traveled over the Columbia River near the mouth of the Umatilla while they

were with us.

There was one man and his mother in the train, and one night we camped on

Willow Creek. This man's name was Morgan and he was in the habit of picking

up ail the chains, picks, shovels, etc. he came across until his team was

completely broken down. We had to pull his wagon up all the hills, so that

night we appointed a committee, I being one of them, to inform him that he

must throw out some of those things or we would not help him any more. He

refused to do so. Then I told his mother to get into my wagon, and if I

reached my destination she would too. She said no, it would be too much

trouble. Then a Mr. Person spoke up and said she could ride in his wagon

part of the time. She refused the second offer so we had to proceed without

them.

By this time provisions were getting scarce and we had no time to waste. Im-

mediately after we left them we came to a mountain that took 14 yoke of oxen

to pull up one wagon. The side of the mountain was full of Indians, some on

foot and some on horses, watching us get up the mountain. After we all got

to the top we proceeded on our journey, and before night Morgan passed us

driving his oxen without the wagon. He had sent his mother and provisions

and other things with Indians down to the mouth of the Deschutes River. When

we reached there a man by the name of Coile went down on the bank of the

river and found the old lady in a little tent with the bedding and everything

else she had left was on the forward wheels of the wagon. He took her in his

wagon and hauled her to The Dalles where she found her son.

Deschutes River was quite deep and when we crossed the Indians acted as

guides. We put a rope around the forward oxens' horns, as the route was

somewhat crooked. After I crossed another team started and it got about

half way across and refused to go any farther. I got on my mule and took

my good yoke of leaders and drove in and hitched them on ahead and pulled

them out. In that way we had quite a time getting the oxen turned around

in the water as it was very swift. The people in the wagon were so dizzy

they could hardly sit in the seat. The Old Methodist National Bldg. was

still standing when we reached The Dalles. But there was no white people

except one Catholic priest amongst the Indians. There we left our wagons

and hired Indians to take us to the Cascades in canoes.

We went on down the river and got a small flat boat and brought it to the

foot of the Cascades, and packed everything on our backs to this boat, the

distance being about 5 miles. We then went on down in the boat which leaked

so bad we had to keep three or four men bailing all the time to keep it from

sinking.

At the foot of Cape Horn Mts. is where the last bite of bread was eaten in

the train. I had been providing provisions for part of the members in the

train for nearly 300 miles. That night we came to where we could get plenty

to eat again. The first house we came to after crossing the plains was at

Washougal and the next one we came to was at Ft. Scott. The man's name was

White (4).

When we got to the Cascades we learned how the Indians made their beds. The

shore was very rocky so they would throw out the rocks from a place big

enough to lay in and make the sand level and that was their resting place.

Before leaving The Dalles I hired an Indian at my own expense to drive the cat-

tle down the trail as I had about 1/3 of the stock in the train. We crossed

the cattle about 10 miles below the Cascades and next day we landed at Cas.

with stock.

The next winter I went to work at a sawmill owned by a man named Parker (5) about

mile and a half from where we landed. He paid me $5.00 per day and I agreed

to work that winter for him. A few days later I was offered work at another

mill at Milwaukie (6) at $7.00 per day. But as I had promised to work that

winter, I stuck to my job. After we were at Parker's a few days a man and

myself took a skift (skiff?) and went to Vancouver for provisions. We got

plenty of flour and 30 pounds of pork from soldiers’ rations. A barrel of

thin syrup and a little butter so strong that it cured me from eating butter.

We got the flour from the Hudson Bay Co. six miles above Vancouver. After

we left there a heavy wind came up and while we was crossing the river the

water kept splashing in the boat, and the other man became frightened. He

was pulling and I was steering and he quit, so I said, "Pull, you devil,

pull, or we will go down}" So he pulled like a good fellow till we reached

the bank. Then we pulled on up near the bank till we got to Washougal.

After we passed there a little ways the river became so swift that two In-

dians had to get in and help us out. One got on each side and pulled us

over the worst part. Then we proceeded to where we was going without any

help.

There was only two men in the country that had any stock before we came there.

One was Mr. Parker and the other an old Hudson Bay fellow that came to Van-

couver in 1824. They had drove their cattle off into the rushes over Cape

Horn Mts., and immediately afterwards there came a two-foot snow. The river

froze over and we had nothing to live on, but that flour and syrup that winter.

Although I had five men boarding with me but they was glad to get what we did.

We had bread and molasses for breakfast, and the reverse for dinner and supper.

In the spring I moved in a little log house joining my farm on Columbia Slough (7)

and soon after we all took the Mountain or Camp Fever, and also ran out of

provisions, yet we had good friends. The year before we moved there, there

was a good crop of potatoes raised on the place, and that year there was a

few volunteer potatoes came up. Our friends, the wood rats (8), would dig them

up and bring them in and pile them up in the log cabin and we would steal

them from the rats and make our soup of them. This was our living for some

time. We had no doctor only from the Hudson Bay Co. and he charged $50.00

per trip besides his ferriage which brought his bill to $52.00. Between him

and the wood rats we weathered it through.

 

My farm was then occupied by a man by the name of Webb. He wanted me to buy

the place on account of him going to the mines at Snake River. He was almost

a total stranger and wanted $1000.00 for the place and stock, which consisted

of two yoke of oxen and 3 cows. I finally made a deal with him. I paid $300

down and gave him my note for $700 payable in 18 months. After the bargain

was closed and the note given, he went out and dug up a good-sized bucket

full of money and wanted me to take care of it and also the note I had given

him. I told him "No," and he urged me so that finally I told him I would not

like to have anybody know it was about the house. He said I was the only one

he would like to keep it and that no one would ever know from him that I had

it. The money consisted of Mexican dollars and gold coins. The gold was in

$8.00 and $16.00 pieces called single loons and double loons. He said that

if he never came back I would be that much better off. I did not see him any

more for 18 months, although the mines he spoke of had never been discovered.

Soon after that Capt. Banburgan and Bradfords above the Cascades brought emi-

gration down on a boat to the Cascades and took cattle for pay, and bought

also a large number of cattle and sent them down for me to sell, which gave

me something of a start. I also took jobs of work, times being good. The

old settlers had been to the mines and came back with quite a little money,

They being too good to work after making a fine start. The first summer I

cleared $8.25 per day outside of my commissions on sales. Lumber at that

time was worth $100.00 per thousand feet, and I went to work hauling lumber

from Will's Mill (6) to the river. When I went to work on Monday, I would take

a cedar saw log on my wagon and get it sawed and Saturday when I went home

I would buy a little more lumber. I was in need of a house very bad, so I

went to work and hewed out sills and studding, and cut white fir timber and

made clab boards and shaved them for siding. When I got it up and painted

very few people ever noticed the difference from sawed lumber. At that time

it was the best locking house in the country. I spent a great deal of ny

time boring holes in burnt down trees and burning them up to clear a place

to live on. After I had burnt off a great deal of timber in that way, I

took a notion to cut the timber in saw logs and bring it to Portland, which

consisted of about 15 or 20 buildings at that time. It was expressed along

the river that I was a big fool for trying to get out saw logs when there

wasn't a boat on the river to tow with. But I had quite a number of oxen.

I thought I could put the logs in the Slough and raft them as cheap as I

could burn them.

There came a high water the next summer, and I had got in about 300,000 ft.

and started them for Portland. We kept close around Gattens Point and went

out into the Willamette opposite Sauvies Island. Before starting I went to

Vancouver and got four old government tents, put up masts on the rafts and

stayed them with lines and stretched these tents up for sails. In a little

over four hours we landed in Portland. Soon after this there was quite a

number of other fools in the same business. Logging was carried on all

along the Slough. I then took a contract for quite a large number of logs,

the pay of which was to be part sawing and part money. When I got almost

ready to start the logs to Portland, I received a word that the parties were

broke. So I came to Portland and went to Mr. Mills the contractor (9) and told

him I heard he was broke, and told him I could not make the contract on

those conditions. Then I went back and got my logs ready and brought them

to Portland. So he offered to let me run the mill a month free. I told

him I would not start it for a month's run at all. He then wanted to sell

me the mill. I think I made about four bargains with him before he would

stand to it. I finally bought the mill (10) to save myself on the logs, the

raft being fully 3/4 of a mile long. I ran the mill a while, then sold out

mill (10) and logs, there being $6,000 due me. They kept insisting for me to

wait longer and longer for the money, till I waited six years (10) and had to

take my pay in greenbacks which were only worth 50¢ on the dollar.

Before there was any steamers here and also before this logging contract,

I took up a contract to bring up logs in the winter time. I think they

were to be at Portland some time in January. I was not much acquainted

with currents here and during this time there came a raise of about four

or five feet in the Willamette, and I first thought I was not going to be

able to get the logs to Portland. I came in and told the parties I didn't

think I could quite fulfill my promise on account of the raise in the

river. So they said they would take them any time I could get them here.

I went and bought a coil and a half of rope and spliced it together and

pinned some logs together and made a windlass with a long sweep. I put

that in a boat and would go up stream with it the length of the line.

I put two men on the sweep to make it fast, then go back and take turns at

Captain, and one man to coil up the rope. We brought that up the river

against a pretty strong current, making about 3/4 of a mile a day, and

we reached Portland in 16 days.

In 1857 I went into the merchandise business (10A) in Portland and had about

the largest retail store in town. After running it some time I became

too weakly to attend it, so had to put a man in charge by the name of

Z. N. Stansbury (11). He was elected County Treasurer and four of us went

his bonds (11), and was the defaulter for $ 8,000.00. What I lost in the way

of bonds and goods from the store through him made me the loser of about

$11,000.00. I was so bad with kidney complaint that I was not able to

be out of bed. There was an old doctor by the name of Millard in Port-

land well known to the old settlers. He gave me four large doses of

calomel within about a week. The last dose commenced operating and I

felt my kidneys break loose and drop back about an inch and a half as

near as I could tell, and after that I felt like a new man.

The next spring after this, I think it was in 1859, I fitted up a team

and went East of the mountains. I had made arrangements with Colonel

Rubble (11A)  to take me to The Dalles on his boat, and within about a week after

the arrangements were made he sold out to the 0.R.& N.Co., leaving me out

in the cold. I then chartered the steamer Independence (12) and went to the

Cascades on her. We camped there two weeks and then proceeded on up the

river on a large scow that came down after us. I took a skift (skiff?)

and went up and met them and piloted from there down. Then the whole

company got very good and offered to take me to The Dalles on most any

terms. But I told them I had a conveyance to go up on so we loaded up

and went ahead. We had a good breeze and went up quite rapidly. Some

of the parties on the scow didn't understand the navigating of the scow

very well and was about to drift her into the rocks on the Washington

side. I said a man by the name of Vants to pick up a large 2-ft. plank

that was on board and drop it over the side for a lay board to keep her

from going onto the rocks and then we soon brought her over to Crates

Point (13). There we landed and took our stock and teams off. This was about

3 miles below The Dalles. Then we traveled at leisure, killing prairie.

chickens and broiling them on the coals for our living. From Crates

Point went on to Walla Walla by land, leaving two wagons at The Dalles

and taking two with us. When we arrived at Walla Walla I sold my ox team.

An Indian agent was there for the purpose of protecting the Indians.

Across the river was a tent where the white people sold liquor to the

Indians. They got the Indians drunk and while they were in that dazed

condition they took about 150 or 200 ponies belonging to the Indians,

and took them off down the valley and sold them. This is the way the

Indians were “protected” there.

 After we got to Walla Walla there was going to be a flour mill (13A) raised

which belonged to a man by the name of Reynolds (13A). Captain Black and the

Indian agent owned the ground for the millsite. So the Indian agent was

sent out to invite the men to the raising of the mill. They learned that

I was there so they told him not to come back without me, After he found

me and I promised to come he said they had a certain duty for me to pe -

form. I asked him what it was and he said it was to keep Reynolds in a

good humor (13A). The men worked out in the hot sun where it was almost un-

bearable to be and Mr. Reynolds’ head got so hot that it kept him in a

bad humor. He used such abusive language that several of the men quit

work and left. So I got the rest of them in a cluster and told them not

to get angry at whatever he might say. I soon discovered that he was

completely overcome with the heat. So I told him to tell me which way

the timbers went and he did, so we soon had the mill up. Although we had

no sawed timbers to put in, all being hewed, even the braces and studding,

it twisted around so in the hot sun that it’ was hard to get together, but

finally it came out all right.

After that we visited Whitman's Station, expecting to find the handsomest

place in the valley. Although a pretty fair place, the site selected for

it was between two hills that came near together right in the Indians’ pass

way. There was two small pair of millstones dressed out of the rock found

there to put up a small mill where the Indians could get their corn ground.

Then we returned to Portland and I bought a sawmill at Vancouver (14), and ex-

pected to get the government trade. I kept it for a while but as I charged

them the same as I did everybody else I lost their trade. I built a grist

mill to it (15), and it afterwards burnt (15) without any insurance. I then built

a grist and sawmill where the Hudson Bay Co. had theirs (16). After running

that quite a while (16A), it became flooded with high water and several of us

took the notion to go to boating.

We had a meeting. Myself, Farnsworth and J. D. Biles were elected direct-

ors. It was almost all thrown onto my shoulders. So I went out to Salmon

Creek and rented a mill (17). I took some men out to dig up the trees with long

roots on one side for the bottom timbers of the boat. I would go out one

morning and saw till the next evening and bring a load in with me. Farns-

worth was a good builder and he wanted to build the boat. Then I had to

take charge of the planking under his directions. The O.R.&N. Co. made fun

of us, they having two large boats brought here from the East. I made a

contract to have the machinery made in Portland. We sent to California for

the silders and a few other things, and they were sent up to be finished

here. The boiler was too light and before we got through it broke up.

Then I hired a young man by the day whose name was Dehuff, to finish up the

machinery. I got the shop for him to work in, and when it was finished we

started to put it in the boat.

About that time there was an organization of the Peoples' Transportation

Co. and we gave them the job of raising the machinery. When it was com-

pleted we had the fastest boat on the river. Her name was the E. D. BAKER (18).

Then I commenced getting out timbers for another boat above the Cascades (18A).

I hauled the timber to my own mill and sawed it and sent it above the Cas-

cades. We finished that boat and called her the IRIS. We also had an-

other boat above Deschutes River. At last a dispute arose between the two

companies, to see which would have the route on the Columbia. We commenced

cutting rates, till we carried passengers from Portland to The Dalles for

50¢. Both companies run in debt until Mr. Ladd made a proposition to com-

promise. We gave them two steamboats and freight boats on the Columbia,

keeping the E.D. BAKER, and they gave us 7 steamboats and freight boats on

the Willamette and $10,000 in cash for us to get off of the Columbia and

they got off of the Willamette.

Then we went to work and put in a breakwater at Oregon City (19) 1½  mi. in

length and built a basin at the falls to run into. Then we built a shoot

to get the freight down to the boats below. I stayed in that business

several years and sold out. After I sold out, stock commenced running down

till it got to about 30¢ on the dollar, and then a man by the name of Holla-

day bought out the whole thing,

I then came to Portland and went into the furniture business. I built me a

large store (18B) and had it all quite nicely furnished and the big fire of 1873

burnt it up and nearly everything in it, without any insurance. Then I had

to recruit a few years before I could do much more. A few years after that

I put up some brick buildings (20) and also some wooden ones, which I have yet

except what I have given to the heirs.

The year of 1899, Nov. 29, about four months ago, I commenced to build a

new residence (21), and expect to put quite a few men to work making more im-

provements in a few days. I have been sought after for some time forging

wills and deeds (22) for a large amount of my property. I have stood them off

so far and some others have stood the penalty of the law.

The grist stones were sent to Fort Vancouver from Great Britain by ship

base of cement made from coral shipped in from the Hawaiian Islands. The

is in better repair. Both will be restored eventually to working order…..

We start with the 1852 survey map. Where the road to Vancouver meets the path that is now Columbia Boulevard we see Captain Love’s house, in the northeast corner of the intersection.

At that time it was the only house in what is now the Piedmont neighborhood. There seemed to have been basically two roads or paths from Portland to Vancouver. The main one is the County Road and there is a secondary path, which is probably older. Love built his house next to the county road, and John Rankin built his house half a mile to the west, closer to the old path. On the cutout below we see the two houses (as black dots) in rectangle gray areas that indicate improved farm lands.

There is an interesting article in the Morning Oregonian of April 26, 1897. It is an interview with Lewis Love about the period, in 1852-1853.

Captain Love built and occupied the first house on the main road between Portland and Vancouver. It was he who ran that road through to the Columbia Slough, and he was supervisor of the road for a long time.

And:

I moved away in 1855 and rented my home there to John Rankin, who had built a house two years after our arrival, about half a mile west and off the road.

There is another interview in the Morning Oregonian of December 4, 1900, under the inviting title “Mr. Love’s Stories”.

When Mr. Love started to farm on Columbia Slough Indian dances were frequent. Portland was undreamt of.

I somehow doubt the indian dances part. The interview goes on about how Lewis Love got into logging.

It was costing me a great deal to burn up my timber while clearing. I decided to try rafting to Portland. I put about 20,000 logs in the stream during high water in the Winter of 1852. I then built upright posts and yards on one end of the raft and sailed to Portland. A good strong breeze carried us up the river. We made the trip in about four hours. Another time I came near being caught in a pinch on a contract to deliver logs to the mills in Portland. The water dropped down and a strong current was running in the river. We had to work our way up from the head of the Sauvies Island Slough by an improvised capstan, or windlass, the end of the rope being attached to any convenient object along the shore while the windlass was worked taking up the coil of rope. If I remember rightly we were 16 days getting to the city.

Multnomah County did not exist until December 22, 1854. Before that we were in Clackamas County. The Will

On January 5, 1899 Lewis Love made his final will and testament, which I will (reluctantly) retype in full. It has a useful list of the real property owned and it lists all the heirs. It shows how careful Lewis Love was to make sure this document was known to be his real and only will. As the end of the Manuscript indicates, he had to deal with forged wills and with challenges of his wife’s will after she died. The will also indicates that in all the years he never sold any real estate, he only bought more. The parcels in sections 10, 11, 14, and 16 were 640 acres at the time of the donation land claim, and they were 757 acres at the time of the testament, mostly by extending the original DLC to the west and to the north over the Slough, by buying parts of the Switzler DLC up to Force’s Lake.

Remember that Lewis Love died in 1903. It must have been very frustrating for the heirs that they had to wait for distribution until 1907. The will, and the codicil that follows the will, have been taken from the Love v. Walker et al. lawsuit in the Oregon Supreme Court (Pacific Reporter, 1911).

On February 26, 1902, Lewis Love added a codicil to his will, and one that caused a great deal of consternation and a long-lasting lawsuit after his death.

I, Lewis Love of Portland, Oregon, do make this codicil to my will. I hereby expressly confirm my last will, dated January 5, 1899, excepting in so far as the distribution of my property is changed by this codicil. First. I hereby will, decree and declare that the devise or legacy to my daughter, Mary C. Stafford in my said will, shall be for her sole and separate use, independent of her husband at all times, and that at her death the said devise or legacy to her shall go to her children, share and share alike. Second. I hereby will, decree and declare that the devise or legacy, in my said will, to my son, Fred D. Love, shall be for his sole and separate use, independent of his wife at all times, and that at his death the said devise or legacy to him shall go to his children, share and share alike. Third. Second. I hereby will, decree and declare that the devise or legacy in my said will to my son, Green C. Love shall be for his sole and separate use, independent of his wife at all times, and that in case of his death without lawful issue, born alive and living at the time of his death, then the said devise or legacy to him shall belong and go to the remaining devisees of my said will in proportion as they hold of the shares or parts of my said will. Lastly. I declare this is a codicil to my will, and that this is the only codicil that I have made, and I hereby declare my said will of date January 5, 1899, to be my last will and testament and also hereby re-affirm the same in every particular, except a modified by this codicil, which codicil is to be attached to the sad will. Witness my hand and sea this 26th day of February, 1902. [Signed] Lewis Love. [Seal.]

The codicil does two things. It protects the estate against the scheming husbands and wives of Mary, Fred, and Green and makes sure the property only travels in a straight genealogical line, without reverting to inlaws and other deplorables. In the second place it singles out Green C. Love, who was married in 1876 and had five children. In 1896, however, both his wife and all his children had died. He remarried in 1901 with a bride he had found through a personal ad, which was a method and a resulting bride the Captain did not approve of. So again the special provision had be added to the codicil to make sure the property did not go to outsiders. As we shall see this codicil resulted in lawsuits and court proceedings that lasted more than ten years. More about this later in this chapter.

William Love: The Dairy Man

The oldest son, William Ulysses Love, was born in 1838 in Missouri. He traveled with his parents to Oregon in 1849. He first married Temperance Shaver on November 2, 1854, who died March 1, 1858 after giving birth to two children, and then on March 10, 1859 he married 14 year old Sarah Catherine Windle, with whom he had an additional seven children. In the 1870’s William Love ran a successful dairy business from his home on the Columbia Slough. In the Oregonian of September 1871 we find advertisements for that business.

Even earlier, in 1860 when he was still in his early twenties, William Love acquired a parcel of land of more than 150 acres. I do not know where he got the more than one thousand dollars to pay for it, but let’s review the parcel we are talking about.

On March 10, 1866 the United States registered a military warrant for 160 acres to Charles Vincent, for his service in Private Company H, 2nd Regiment of U.S. Dragoons, in some so-called Apache Indian Disturbance. Charles Vincent immediately transferred the land to Robert S. Maxey. Here is the patent.

https://drive.google.com/open?id=1OUOOb58IKpdXVW8GebZXacBx45Y_f0dw

The precise description of the land is

The fractional South East quarter of Section Nine and the Fractional South West quarter of Section Ten in Township One North of Range One East in the District of lands subject to sale at Oregon City, Oregon, containing one hundred and Fifty two acres and one hundredth of an acre.

In terms of modern streets, the land is bounded by Kilpatrick to the north, Lombard to the south, the I-5 to the east, and (approximately) Delaware to the west. In other words, a large chunk of current Kenton. On December 28, 1860 Robert S. Maxey sold the 152.01 acres to William Love for one thousand and twenty five dollars.

https://drive.google.com/open?id=1d0DwA318z6u_lHuJCiMru_CyE84ScvE-

The dates on the deed are interesting. The Military Donation act was passed in 1855, and presumably Charles Vincent claimed his donation shortly after that. Then Robert Maxey sold to William Love in 1860, even though the official warrant was not issued until 1866. If we go over the various homesteads and donation land grants mentioned in this book we see that many of the warrants and patents were not registered until 1866. This delay is not unusual, as Vaughan (2014, p. 45) explains.

The GLO district land offices were required to certify entitlement to a land patent by issuance of a final certificate upon satisfaction of all statutory requirements by the entryman or purchaser.

And the year 1866 is of special significance. Again from Vaughan (2014, p. 45)

The issuance of land patents was greatly curtailed during the turbulent Civil War years of 1861 to 1865 – delaying most donation land claim patents until after the war. 

In the case of the Maxey-Love transaction there was even more delay. The deed was signed in 1860 in the County of Clark, Territory of Washington, in the presence of William Kelly, Clerk of the District Court. Mr. Kelly basically notarized the signing of the deed, using an oath by a Mr. John Hexler that the person who appeared before him was indeed Robert Maxey. This may not have been enough to actually file the deed with Multnomah County, and in 1877 Roger Greene, associate justice of the Washington Supreme Court, testified that William Kelly was indeed a clerk at that time, and that the laws of Washington authorized him to notarize the deed. In addition, in 1882, a Mr. McConnell, the current clerk and  record keeper for the district court, testified that William Kelly was who he said he was, and that his signature was indeed his true signature. So this was 22 years after the original signing. Moreover, as we shall see, it was three years after the death of William Love.

Over the years, William Love became a thriving  and well-respected citizen. In the 1870 census the family lived in the Willamette Precinct with six children and two laborers. In 1875 William was appointed Supervisor of Multnomah County road district 3. In 1876 he was appointed Judge of Elections in the Willamette District. He also served on the school board for the district. But all was not well. Below are two articles from the Morning Oregonian, the first from February 12, 1879 and the second from the next day, and one from the Willamette Farmer of February 14. William Love committed suicide by drowning himself in the Columbia Slough. “... he had rid himself of his bodily ills by casting himself into the river.” There is obviously no clear diagnosis of his ailment in these articles from 1879, but the officiating clergyman mentioned that “a protracted illness of nearly two years, accompanied by occasional paroxysms of internal distress, had so far unbalanced his mind as to constitute him periodically insane.”

William died on February 11, 1879. One week later, on February 17, his father Lewis Love was appointed as administrator of the estate. For those who are really interested, here is a link to the 143-page probate file from the Multnomah County Court.

As is usually the case with probate files, it is a genuine treasure trove of information. It has appraisals and descriptions of personal and real property, receipts for various transactions, debts of the estate, letters, court orders, and much more. I will include copies of some of the pages, with some comments. First, there is a nice description of the real property.

This is still the original Robert Maxey DLC. Ulery et al valued the land at $ 2,500. Lewis Love asked permission of the court to sell the land by auction in three pieces: the western half, the western half of the eastern half, and the eastern half of the eastern half. And this is exactly what happened. On February 11, 1882 the western half was sold to William Showers, sheriff of Multnomah County, for $ 1,634.10. At the same auction the western half of the eastern half was sold to Charles Sitton for $ 798.04. It seems that something was wrong with the Showers transaction. On May 15, 1882 there was a second auction, where Joseph Buchtel, sheriff of Multnomah County, bought the western half for $ 3,192.20. The eastern half of the eastern half, which was the part where William Love had his house and business, and the part adjoining the Lewis Love DLC, was not sold, at least not yet.

Here is the final account of the administrator. We see the land sales, totalling $ 3,192.20 + $ 798.04 = $ 3,990.24. There are other items on the “money received” side that are  interesting. There were 670 cords of wood, appraised at $ 700. There were 500 bushels of potatoes, appraised at 60 cents a bushel. They had to be sold rather quickly, otherwise they would sprout. There were six adult cows, appraised at $ 25 per cow, 3 two-year old cows at $ 15, and 3 one-year old cows at $ 7.50. On the “money paid” side we see more than $ 250 paid to neighbor John Hotts, who plays a role in many of the chapters of this book. The $ 408 paid to Antoine Masley was the balance for “work and labor” performed at the request of William Love during the years 1875-1879, at a salary of $ 30 per month. We also see $ 643.98 paid to the Oregon and Washington Trust and Investment Company. This is for a mortgage of originally $ 600 on his land that William Love got in 1877. The biannual payments were about $ 77, and these payments were three years in arrears in 1882. The balance of the account was distributed to Sarah Love and her six sons (or to their guardians, if they were still minors in 1882). Each of them got $ 371.53. There were only six sons left, because William Albert Love died in 1880, at the age of twenty. The administrator Lewis Love received $ 216.90.

Sarah Love continued to live on the homestead until 1897. Then she died.

Mary C. Love: Nurse

Mary C. Love and Nathan Stafford

Mary C. Love was born in Missouri on April 25, 1842. She crossed the plains with her parents and arrived in Multnomah in 1849. On September 14, 1856, when she was 14 years old, she married George W. Shafer (or Schaeffer), who was 30 years old at the time, and in the grocery store business with her father. George Shaeffer died the next year, and Mary married Nathan D. Stafford in 1859, when he was 33 years old and she was 17, and already two years a widow. O tempora ! O mores !

From then on, we can follow Mary through the successive federal censuses and city directories and see what the composition of her household is. Around 1880 she is listed as a nurse or midwife, while Nathan is a carpenter. In 1860 Mary has given birth to a daughter Emeline, at the census date 7 months old. In 1870 Emeline is 8, and she are joined by Leona (6), Matilda (4), and Ulysses Grant (1). In 1880 we find Ida (13), Louis G. (11), Nancy M. (9), Sarah R. (7), Delia (5), Lena (3), and Geo (2 months). There is no 1890 census, and in 1900 there are no new children, and we have Lena (22), Lewis (31), and Nellie C (34) living at home. Nathan Stafford died in 1903.

In the 1910 census Mary has moved in with her daughter Alice (then Alice L. Preacher) and in 1920 she lives with her other daughter Nancy (then Nancy M. Finke). She dies August 14, 1927 at 360 E. Lombard Street (now 65 NE Lombard), 85 years old. From the eulogies it seems that the surviving children are Emma J. Walker, Alsistres L. Peacher, Lewis G. Stafford, Nancy M. Finke,  Sedelia P. Hohmann, all of Portland, Oregon, Sarah R. Anderson of Orchards, Washington, and Lena G. Richmond of Ardenwald, Oregon. Note that Emma is Emeline, the oldest daughter, born in 1860.

I have some special feelings for Mary and Nathan, because I live on Stafford Street. Snyder (1979, p 208) says

The original dedication of this street was in the University Park neighborhood. If, as we believe, the name was chosen by the Methodists who developed University Park, it is probably a namesake for Charles Lewis Stafford, who was president of Iowa Wesleyan University 1891-1899.

Nice try, but no cigar. There seems to have been a Stafford Street in South Saint John’s, which was renamed out of existence around 1908. There is a small section of  Stafford Street on the border of University Park, between Woolsey and Olin, at the south line of Mock’s Addition, but it was originally called Syracuse Street, and it was renamed to Stafford Street in 1918. I do not see a Stafford Street in the original 1891 plat maps for University Park. There is one in the original plats for Loveleigh, Lovewood, and Swinton from around 1908. And then around 1910-1920 there was some additional renaming and Stafford Street came to run east from Union through Farrell’s Addition and Farrell’s Second Addition until it ran into Lombard and stopped.

Initially, Fred and his father seemed to have had a fairly normal relationship. Fred worked in his father’s saw mill, and was captain on his father’s steamboats. There may have been some minor conflicts, caused by incidents such as the one reported in the Vancouver Independent of December 7, 1882.

But the relationship went definitely south after the death of Nancy in 1892, and at breakneck speed. The reason is probably that there were two different versions of Nancy’s wills circulating. I will discuss this in great detail in the section on the fake will in this chapter, but for now it suffices to know that in one of the wills Nancy left $ 100 to each of her sons and daughters, and the rest of the estate to Lewis Love while in the other will she left $ 100 to Lewis Love and the rest of the estate to her sons and daughters. And remember that it was the Lewis and Nancy Love Donation Land Claim, which means that legally she was the owner of 320 acres. And development had started in the area, with Good Morning Addition, Piedmont, Woodlawn, Saratoga, Lochinvar all having been platted in 1892. Captain Lewis Love was an old hand at lawsuits, and he did not take kindly to the fact that Fred Love sued him in trying to throw out the first will, favorable to Lewis, and replace it by the second will, favorable to the offspring, and thus to Fred. It seems Lewis did not take half measures, and he had Fred arrested for insanity in November 1892. From the Oregonian of July 15, 1893:

Ten years later there was another “son against father” case, but this time Fred was the father. The son in this case was Royal Frederick Love.
