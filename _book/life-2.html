History of the Piedmont Neighborhood 1850-1920

4 Life

In November 1899 Captain Lewis Love sat down with his granddaughter Harriet LaVelle Peacher and dictated a manuscript with the details of his trek across the plains from Missouri to Oregon, and about his subsequent activities and enterprises in Oregon and Washington. There are nine nicely cleaned up and retyped pages. A second seventeen-page manuscript, by Captain Love’s great-granddaughter Clara Love Stone goes through Captain Love’s manuscript and provides many additional details and references. These manuscripts are the holy grail of Lewis Love scholarship. I will refer to them in this chapter as the Love Manuscript and the Love Commentary, with a capital M and a capital C. They are reproduced here in total, because we will use them as the starting point for much of the chapter. As you will see, a great deal of attention is paid to Love’s travels on the Oregon Trail, which probably was the most exciting period of his life, although surely not the most profitable one. In the text we have incorporated the underlined and numbered references to the Love Commentary by Ms. Stone, which follows the Love Manuscript.

4.1 Manuscript of Captain Lewis Love

_(Commenced writing Nov. 1, 1899 and finished Nov. 29, 1899. Dictated to his_


_granddaughter, Harriet LaVelle Peacher )_


_PREFACE_


_Captain Love was born in New York, Chautauqua Co., June 12, 1818. After_


_living there till he was 15 years of age he moved to Fox River Co., Illinois_


_in the year 1833. From there he moved to Missouri in 1845 and crossed the_


_plains to come West in 1849. His mother was part Pennsylvania Dutch and part_


_Yankee; she was born in New York and her name before she was married was Jane_


_Demont (deMott). She was the mother of 6 boys and 2 girls all of which are_


_dead (Nov. 1899) except Captain Lewis Love._


_Mrs. Love, Captain Lewis Love's wife was born in Tennessee, near a little_


_town called Spy and moved to Illinois in 1833 where she met Mr. Love and they_


_were married in 1836. Mrs. Love's Mother's name was Edwards before she was_


_married and Mrs. Love's name was Griffith. Her Mother was from Tennessee. She_


_was born before the Revolution and has been dead 70 years._


_There was born to Captain Lewis Love and wife six boys and 2 girls, namely_


_Mary C. Love, Melinda Love, Green Love, Lewis Love, Fred Love, William Love,_


_Jeremiah and Joseph beside four children which they raised but never adopted._


_There was three boys drowned, namely William Love and --- and --- (Names_


_not given in the manuscript for the latter two)_


_CIRCUMSTANCES CROSSING PLAINS_


_The train which Captain Lewis Love crossed the plains in started from_


_<span style="text-decoration:underline;">Lawrence Co., Mo</span>. (1), and when they got thirty miles north of Ft. Scott,_


_they came to high water called the Osage River._


_There was only three families together at that time, so they went to_


_work putting in timbers to make a raft to cross on about 2:00 P.M. Two_


_Cherokee Indians rode up and asked us what we were doing. They were on_


_their way to the gold fields in California. We told them we were putting_


_in timbers to build a raft to cross the high water, so they said for us to_


_turn our teams out and their train would come up that evening with 35_


_wagons and 85 men. So we waited till next morning, then went to work_


_building the raft._


_We got it all together in good shape in a stream called <span style="text-decoration:underline;">Sugar Creek (2)</span>, this_


_being backwater from the Osage River. We floated down to the Osage so as_


_to be able to cross both streams at once. There we found the river to be_


_very swift, too much so to hold against the current. Then we wondered how_


_we was going to cross. Finally Capt, Love suggested that we take<span style="text-decoration:underline;"> grapevines (3)</span>_


_and make ropes. Two or three of the Indians kept interfering till the main_


_body drove the others off and told them to stay until it was time for them_


_to cross. Capt. Love took one man with him and they pounded the vines till_


_they could be beat to make a cable. They made a cable to reach a quarter_


_of a mile and forked it down near the raft to keep it from swinging around_


_so as to pull across straight. We then swam over and took ropes to pull_


_us in the direction we wanted to go. We put one wagon on the raft at a_


_time and each trip the raft would sink knee deep till we struck the swift-_


_est part of the river, then the raft would rise on top and float from there_


_over. We commenced taking things across at 2:00 P.M. and that night at_


_10:00 P.M. everything was over safe. From that on the Cherokees became so_


_much attached to me they would ride three or four miles to see me._


_Soon afterwards Cholera set in and it was not uncommon for four or five_


_cases to be reported in camp, the Cherokees being about the heaviest suf-_


_ferers according to the number, and they died off very fast._


_The Ninth Regiment that crossed the plains that year took them in tow and_


_kept them for several hundred miles until they were able to take care of_


_the teams themselves, then they were discharged. And the last I saw of_


_them was on South Pass in the Rocky Mts. They rode past us four miles and_


_drove back to bid me farewell._


_Not long after we left the Osage River we came to a bluff where we had to_


_let the wagons down by means of trees and ropes. Some of the trees had_


_gashes cut in them fully two inches from letting down so many wagons. We_


_had not traveled far when an axle broke in one of the wagons so we went_


_along up the canyon a ways till we came to some ash trees. We hewed a_


_piece of ash down and made another axle._


_One morning while we were camping at a place called Potowatammy, I went_


_out to hunt up the cattle. When I found them there were 41 missing. The_


_word came that Indians were seen driving them away up the road, The man_


_that was with me wanted to go that way to find them. I told him, No, it_


_was a hook (hoax) to keep us from finding them. I told him I was too well_


_acquainted with the Indians to believe they would steal my cattle. So we_


_struck across the plains about four miles to a high ridge which had quite_


_a stream of water at the foot of it. There I saw three squaws riding across_


_the plains. I hailed them, and as I could talk a little of their language,_


_I asked them if they had seen any cattle. So they motioned up over the_


_ridge where there was three men herding them and just ready to start off_


_with them. I wanted to know of them what they were doing with those cattle,_


_and they said they was sent back by a train about four miles ahead to gather_


_them up. I told them it was no such a thing for my cattle were all branded_


_and there was the brand to show for itself. They had 13 head of oxen of_


_mine which would have left me only one to work. When we got to Ft. Carney_


_(Kearney) I took a man by the name of Miller in the train to assist. The_


_rule was that two men was to stand guard every night and when it came my_


_turn he never would let me go but he would take my place. When we got_


_pretty well to Ft. Laramie there was five men and a wagon came into the_


_train. One of the men was a great big stout fellow and to let him tell_


_it he could whip all the Indians on the plains. They took a  notion one_


_day, quite a number, that he was a coward and along towards night he was_


_pointing out how many pony tracks there was. So they thought they would_


_play a joke on him. It was his night to be on duty, and of course if any_


_Indians came around they knew he would shoot them. One old man in their_


_wagon called "Pap" on account of his age told him he had been an old front-_


_iersman and would like to load his gun for him. So it was loaded with a_


_paper wad, After supper the two men went out on guard. The second man had_


_been informed of the joke so he was all ready for them. A while later five_


_men went out, three of whom had guns, and went around in behind the men and_


_two of them commenced patting on their knees to draw his attention. His_


_bravery left him very suddenly and, making the best speed possible for the_


_camp, told them the Indians were after him and he had come for help. Next_


_morning quite a crowd gathered around in one wagon, and were talking about_


_the Indians' attack and one person asked him how many Indians he really_


_thought there was. He said there was 25 or 30 in front and they occupied a_


_piece of ground the shape of a flatiron, and back of this number their heads_


_stuck up as thick as they could be._


_After we crossed South Platte, apast Ft. Laramie, two or three days' ride,_


_we stopped to noon one day. While we were eating, two men came back a-_


_horseback. They were in haste and wanted us to drive on as they wanted help._


_The time came for the train to start and several more trains drove up and a_


_German man belonging to one of the trains was very much excited over the re-_


_port of the Indians and went on to tell how they would kill us. I went up_


_to him and put my hand on his shoulder and told him he must not talk that way_


_because he was frightening the women and children. He says, "You are right",_


_and you could hardly hear another word out of him after that. When we got_


_straightened around ready to start, we traveled about two miles, when we_


_found we had to turn around and come back about five miles. We started down_


_Ash Canyon. After going down some distance the same two men came riding_


_back further up on the offside of the teams, yelling for the women and_


_children to get into the wagons, that the Indians were advancing. The men_


_reported 1500 to 2000 Indians. They came on around where I was and I stepped_


_out and told them the same as I told the German man, and if they didn't stop_


_I would knock them off their horses. As soon as they passed on, I took my_


_gun and went to see what there was over the hill. ‘Iwo women went with me._


_We went to the top of the hill. There we stopped and estimated about 75 or_


_80 Indians out of the 1500 or 2000. ‘Then we went back down the hill and met_


_our teams. We then reported the number of Indians. A great many would not_


_believe us on account of the large number being reported before. We then_


_drove on out to the mouth of the canyon, having about 80 horsemen in front,_


_well-armed. When we got down to where the Indians were we rode on down_


_amongst them and found everything very peaceful after all the scare. It_


_was a remnant of the Sioux tribe who had just been engaged in a battle with_


_a tribe called the Blackfeet. I wanted to know of those fellows after the_


_trains had stopped, why they had made so much fuss about the Indians. They_


_said one Indian made a mark across the road in the dust. Those were the_


_first Indians we had seen since leaving the Potowatammy tribe, so many_


_Indians had left the trail on account of the Whites and we only saw a few_


_buffalo._


_Close to Independence Rock, which was literally covered with names, quite a_


_number of people had gathered together. One train of 52 wagons and car-_


_riages drove up which took passengers and freight. That train held a meet~_


_ing and decided to throw out 1,000 pounds of freight, some offering a dol-_


_lar a pound to get it hauled through. They went to work unloading the freight._


_Amongst other things there was quite a large quantity of powder. They took_


_it and poured it in a circle around quite a large piece of ground out of the_


_kegs; then put a fuse to it and burnt the circle in that way, and a great_


_many articles that was supposed to have been thrown out were shipped in other_


_Wagons._


_After that we came to a great many places where wagons and buggies were left_


_standing different places on the plains. One instance I unhitched from a_


_2-horse wagon and left it standing with the cover on. I asked the people if_


_they objected to my leaving mine and taking the other one. They said no, I_


_was welcome to it, so I changed the load and left my wagon standing with the_


_cover on._


_It was a very common thing for the oxen to get sore feet. So we would take_


_a piece of skin from a dead ox and sew it around the sore feet to keep the_


_sand from getting into them. Nothing more of much importance occurred till_


_we struck the Black Hills. Two men had gone ahead to find a camping place_


_at the foot of the Black Hills, and when they got there they reported no_


_grass to be found -- nothing but sagebrush. They said we would have to tie_


_our cattle up with nothing to eat. I got on my mule and asked if any man_


_would go with me to hunt grass. It was in the P.M. We struck out for a_


_high rocky ridge. When we got about 2/3 of the way to it, the Irishman_


_would not go any further. He said there was no use. I went about a mile_


_and then had to get off my muie and walk, it was so rocky. I went on over_


_the ridge and down to the creek bottom and found some grass and brush, etc._


_I went back and took the cattle over, which gave them 2/3 of a feed. A_


_watchman stayed on the hill overnight. Next morning I went over with the_


_rest to drive the cattle back and then I told them about there being quite_


_a number of Indians camping along the creek. They thought I hadn't been_


_down there, but after a while they found the camps as I had told then._


_Then we drove on a half day and stopped to noon and a man by the name of_


_Dotworthy and myself went down on the ridges to see if we could find any-_


_thing to shoot. There we picked up a chunk which afterwards proved to be_


_gold. We looked at.it awhile and then threw it down and left it. No doubt_


_the piece was worth $1000.00._


_When we reached Bare River Hills, there we saw the first snow and straw-_


_berry blossoms stick up to the edge of the snow. When we got down to Snake_


_River, we found plenty of Indians. I used to practice lifting with them._


_I had one wagon that was pretty heavily loaded. I would turn my back to_


_the wheel and take hold of the spokes and then motion for some of the big_


_Indians to come and lift it. But I never found but one that could lift it._


_Then the Indians would point to me and say I was so much stronger than they_


_were. When we came to Salmon Falls the Indians came to us every day and_


_wanted to swap salmon for shirts. It didn't make any difference how ragged_


_they were just so they could get them. Sometimes they would get a little_


_sulky because we wouldn't trade with them. We traveled all the way down on_


_the South side of Snake River and the first country we found that appeared_


_to be muddy was along Powder River. When we started over Grand Round Valley,_


_I thought it the most beautiful country we had found yet. There I saw the_


_largest bunch of Indian ponies I had ever seen. There were fully 2000 in_


_the number._


_When we started up into the Blue Mountains there for the first time we came_


_into tall timber, and crossing Grand Round (Ronde) River you could see salmon_


_darting in every direction. After crossing the first mountain we found some_


_very rich land. When we crossed the first mountain before we crossed the_


_river there was about a dozen Indians traveled with us for two weeks. I had_


_a spring wagon with me, which the Indians had never seen before. Every night_


_they would deposit a package of some kind in that wagon and would remove it_


_again every morning, and would talk about the wagon and appeared to be very_


_much pleased. They would camp at night and build a fire, and lay two tent_


_poles parallel on the ground; then they would form on each side and sit down_


_with sticks in their hands and commence singing. The first Indian would have_


_a stick sharpened to a point at each end, just long enough so he could hold_


_it in his hands without it being seen. Then he would throw it from one hand_


_to the other and the one that guessed which hand the stick was in got the_


_stick and repeated the same thing. This was their way of gambling. They_


_seemed very much delighted to think they could travel along with us. We_


_traveled over the Columbia River near the mouth of the Umatilla while they_


_were with us._


_There was one man and his mother in the train, and one night we camped on_


_Willow Creek. This man's name was Morgan and he was in the habit of picking_


_up ail the chains, picks, shovels, etc. he came across until his team was_


_completely broken down. We had to pull his wagon up all the hills, so that_


_night we appointed a committee, I being one of them, to inform him that he_


_must throw out some of those things or we would not help him any more. He_


_refused to do so. Then I told his mother to get into my wagon, and if I_


_reached my destination she would too. She said no, it would be too much_


_trouble. Then a Mr. Person spoke up and said she could ride in his wagon_


_part of the time. She refused the second offer so we had to proceed without_


_them._


_By this time provisions were getting scarce and we had no time to waste. Im-_


_mediately after we left them we came to a mountain that took 14 yoke of oxen_


_to pull up one wagon. The side of the mountain was full of Indians, some on_


_foot and some on horses, watching us get up the mountain. After we all got_


_to the top we proceeded on our journey, and before night Morgan passed us_


_driving his oxen without the wagon. He had sent his mother and provisions_


_and other things with Indians down to the mouth of the Deschutes River. When_


_we reached there a man by the name of Coile went down on the bank of the_


_river and found the old lady in a little tent with the bedding and everything_


_else she had left was on the forward wheels of the wagon. He took her in his_


_wagon and hauled her to The Dalles where she found her son._


_Deschutes River was quite deep and when we crossed the Indians acted as_


_guides. We put a rope around the forward oxens' horns, as the route was_


_somewhat crooked. After I crossed another team started and it got about_


_half way across and refused to go any farther. I got on my mule and took_


_my good yoke of leaders and drove in and hitched them on ahead and pulled_


_them out. In that way we had quite a time getting the oxen turned around_


_in the water as it was very swift. The people in the wagon were so dizzy_


_they could hardly sit in the seat. The Old Methodist National Bldg. was_


_still standing when we reached The Dalles. But there was no white people_


_except one Catholic priest amongst the Indians. There we left our wagons_


_and hired Indians to take us to the Cascades in canoes._


_We went on down the river and got a small flat boat and brought it to the_


_foot of the Cascades, and packed everything on our backs to this boat, the_


_distance being about 5 miles. We then went on down in the boat which leaked_


_so bad we had to keep three or four men bailing all the time to keep it from_


_sinking._


_At the foot of Cape Horn Mts. is where the last bite of bread was eaten in_


_the train. I had been providing provisions for part of the members in the_


_train for nearly 300 miles. That night we came to where we could get plenty_


_to eat again. The first house we came to after crossing the plains was at_


_Washougal and the next one we came to was at Ft. Scott. The man's name was_


_<span style="text-decoration:underline;">White </span>(4)._


_When we got to the Cascades we learned how the Indians made their beds. The_


_shore was very rocky so they would throw out the rocks from a place big_


_enough to lay in and make the sand level and that was their resting place._


_Before leaving The Dalles I hired an Indian at my own expense to drive the cat-_


_tle down the trail as I had about 1/3 of the stock in the train. We crossed_


_the cattle about 10 miles below the Cascades and next day we landed at Cas._


_with stock._


_The next winter I went to work at a sawmill owned by a man named <span style="text-decoration:underline;">Parker</span> (5) about_


_mile and a half from where we landed. He paid me $5.00 per day and I agreed_


_to work that winter for him. A few days later I was offered work at <span style="text-decoration:underline;">another</span>_


_<span style="text-decoration:underline;">mill at Milwaukie</span> (6) at $7.00 per day. But as I had promised to work that_


_winter, I stuck to my job. After we were at Parker's a few days a man and_


_myself took a skift (skiff?) and went to Vancouver for provisions. We got_


_plenty of flour and 30 pounds of pork from soldiers’ rations. A barrel of_


_thin syrup and a little butter so strong that it cured me from eating butter._


_We got the flour from the Hudson Bay Co. six miles above Vancouver. After_


_we left there a heavy wind came up and while we was crossing the river the_


_water kept splashing in the boat, and the other man became frightened. He_


_was pulling and I was steering and he quit, so I said, "Pull, you devil,_


_pull, or we will go down}" So he pulled like a good fellow till we reached_


_the bank. Then we pulled on up near the bank till we got to Washougal._


_After we passed there a little ways the river became so swift that two In-_


_dians had to get in and help us out. One got on each side and pulled us_


_over the worst part. Then we proceeded to where we was going without any_


_help._


_There was only two men in the country that had any stock before we came there._


_One was Mr. Parker and the other an old Hudson Bay fellow that came to Van-_


_couver in 1824. They had drove their cattle off into the rushes over Cape_


_Horn Mts., and immediately afterwards there came a two-foot snow. The river_


_froze over and we had nothing to live on, but that flour and syrup that winter._


_Although I had five men boarding with me but they was glad to get what we did._


_We had bread and molasses for breakfast, and the reverse for dinner and supper._


_In the spring I moved in a little log house joining my <span style="text-decoration:underline;">farm on Columbia Slough</span> (7)_


_and soon after we all took the Mountain or Camp Fever, and also ran out of_


_provisions, yet we had good friends. The year before we moved there, there_


_was a good crop of potatoes raised on the place, and that year there was a_


_few volunteer potatoes came up. Our friends, the <span style="text-decoration:underline;">wood rats</span> (8), would dig them_


_up and bring them in and pile them up in the log cabin and we would steal_


_them from the rats and make our soup of them. This was our living for some_


_time. We had no doctor only from the Hudson Bay Co. and he charged $50.00_


_per trip besides his ferriage which brought his bill to $52.00. Between him_


_and the wood rats we weathered it through._


_ _


_My farm was then occupied by a man by the name of Webb. He wanted me to buy_


_the place on account of him going to the mines at Snake River. He was almost_


_a total stranger and wanted $1000.00 for the place and stock, which consisted_


_of two yoke of oxen and 3 cows. I finally made a deal with him. I paid $300_


_down and gave him my note for $700 payable in 18 months. After the bargain_


_was closed and the note given, he went out and dug up a good-sized bucket_


_full of money and wanted me to take care of it and also the note I had given_


_him. I told him "No," and he urged me so that finally I told him I would not_


_like to have anybody know it was about the house. He said I was the only one_


_he would like to keep it and that no one would ever know from him that I had_


_it. The money consisted of Mexican dollars and gold coins. The gold was in_


_$8.00 and $16.00 pieces called single loons and double loons. He said that_


_if he never came back I would be that much better off. I did not see him any_


_more for 18 months, although the mines he spoke of had never been discovered._


_Soon after that Capt. Banburgan and Bradfords above the Cascades brought emi-_


_gration down on a boat to the Cascades and took cattle for pay, and bought_


_also a large number of cattle and sent them down for me to sell, which gave_


_me something of a start. I also took jobs of work, times being good. The_


_old settlers had been to the mines and came back with quite a little money,_


_They being too good to work after making a fine start. The first summer I_


_cleared $8.25 per day outside of my commissions on sales. Lumber at that_


_time was worth $100.00 per thousand feet, and I went to work hauling lumber_


_from <span style="text-decoration:underline;">Will's Mill</span> (6) to the river. When I went to work on Monday, I would take_


_a cedar saw log on my wagon and get it sawed and Saturday when I went home_


_I would buy a little more lumber. I was in need of a house very bad, so I_


_went to work and hewed out sills and studding, and cut white fir timber and_


_made clab boards and shaved them for siding. When I got it up and painted_


_very few people ever noticed the difference from sawed lumber. At that time_


_it was the best locking house in the country. I spent a great deal of ny_


_time boring holes in burnt down trees and burning them up to clear a place_


_to live on. After I had burnt off a great deal of timber in that way, I_


_took a notion to cut the timber in saw logs and bring it to Portland, which_


_consisted of about 15 or 20 buildings at that time. It was expressed along_


_the river that I was a big fool for trying to get out saw logs when there_


_wasn't a boat on the river to tow with. But I had quite a number of oxen._


_I thought I could put the logs in the Slough and raft them as cheap as I_


_could burn them._


_There came a high water the next summer, and I had got in about 300,000 ft._


_and started them for Portland. We kept close around Gattens Point and went_


_out into the Willamette opposite Sauvies Island. Before starting I went to_


_Vancouver and got four old government tents, put up masts on the rafts and_


_stayed them with lines and stretched these tents up for sails. In a little_


_over four hours we landed in Portland. Soon after this there was quite a_


_number of other fools in the same business. Logging was carried on all_


_along the Slough. I then took a contract for quite a large number of logs,_


_the pay of which was to be part sawing and part money. When I got almost_


_ready to start the logs to Portland, I received a word that the parties were_


_broke. So I came to Portland and went to <span style="text-decoration:underline;">Mr. Mills the contractor</span> (9) and told_


_him I heard he was broke, and told him I could not make the contract on_


_those conditions. Then I went back and got my logs ready and brought them_


_to Portland. So he offered to let me run the mill a month free. I told_


_him I would not start it for a month's run at all. He then wanted to sell_


_me the mill. I think I made about four bargains with him before he would_


_stand to it. I finally <span style="text-decoration:underline;">bought the mill</span> (10) to save myself on the logs, the_


_raft being fully 3/4 of a mile long. I ran the mill a while, then <span style="text-decoration:underline;">sold out</span>_


_<span style="text-decoration:underline;">mill</span> (10) and logs, there being $6,000 due me. They kept insisting for me to_


_wait longer and longer for the money, till <span style="text-decoration:underline;">I waited six years</span> (10) and had to_


_take my pay in greenbacks which were only worth 50¢ on the dollar._


_Before there was any steamers here and also before this logging contract,_


_I took up a contract to bring up logs in the winter time. I think they_


_were to be at Portland some time in January. I was not much acquainted_


_with currents here and during this time there came a raise of about four_


_or five feet in the Willamette, and I first thought I was not going to be_


_able to get the logs to Portland. I came in and told the parties I didn't_


_think I could quite fulfill my promise on account of the raise in the_


_river. So they said they would take them any time I could get them here._


_I went and bought a coil and a half of rope and spliced it together and_


_pinned some logs together and made a windlass with a long sweep. I put_


_that in a boat and would go up stream with it the length of the line._


_I put two men on the sweep to make it fast, then go back and take turns at_


_Captain, and one man to coil up the rope. We brought that up the river_


_against a pretty strong current, making about 3/4 of a mile a day, and_


_we reached Portland in 16 days._


_In 1857 I went into the <span style="text-decoration:underline;">merchandise business</span> (10A) in Portland and had about_


_the largest retail store in town. After running it some time I became_


_too weakly to attend it, so had to put a man in charge by the name of_


_<span style="text-decoration:underline;">Z. N. Stansbury</span> (11). He was <span style="text-decoration:underline;">elected County Treasurer and four of us went</span>_


_<span style="text-decoration:underline;">his bonds</span> (11), and was the defaulter for $ 8,000.00. What I lost in the way_


_of bonds and goods from the store through him made me the loser of about_


_$11,000.00. I was so bad with kidney complaint that I was not able to_


_be out of bed. There was an old doctor by the name of Millard in Port-_


_land well known to the old settlers. He gave me four large doses of_


_calomel within about a week. The last dose commenced operating and I_


_felt my kidneys break loose and drop back about an inch and a half as_


_near as I could tell, and after that I felt like a new man._


_The next spring after this, I think it was in 1859, I fitted up a team _


_and went East of the mountains. I had made arrangements with <span style="text-decoration:underline;">Colonel</span>_


_<span style="text-decoration:underline;">Rubble</span> (11A)  to take me to The Dalles on his boat, and within about a week after_


_the arrangements were made he sold out to the 0.R.& N.Co., leaving me out_


_in the cold. I then chartered the <span style="text-decoration:underline;">steamer Independence</span> (12) and went to the_


_Cascades on her. We camped there two weeks and then proceeded on up the_


_river on a large scow that came down after us. I took a skift (skiff?)_


_and went up and met them and piloted from there down. Then the whole_


_company got very good and offered to take me to The Dalles on most any_


_terms. But I told them I had a conveyance to go up on so we loaded up_


_and went ahead. We had a good breeze and went up quite rapidly. Some_


_of the parties on the scow didn't understand the navigating of the scow_


_very well and was about to drift her into the rocks on the Washington_


_side. I said a man by the name of Vants to pick up a large 2-ft. plank_


_that was on board and drop it over the side for a lay board to keep her_


_from going onto the rocks and then we soon brought her over to <span style="text-decoration:underline;">Crates</span>_


_<span style="text-decoration:underline;">Point</span> (13). There we landed and took our stock and teams off. This was about_


_3 miles below The Dalles. Then we traveled at leisure, killing prairie._


_chickens and broiling them on the coals for our living. From Crates_


_Point went on to Walla Walla by land, leaving two wagons at The Dalles_


_and taking two with us. When we arrived at Walla Walla I sold my ox team._


_An Indian agent was there for the purpose of protecting the Indians._


_Across the river was a tent where the white people sold liquor to the_


_Indians. They got the Indians drunk and while they were in that dazed_


_condition they took about 150 or 200 ponies belonging to the Indians,_


_and took them off down the valley and sold them. This is the way the_


_Indians were “protected” there._


_ After we got to Walla Walla there was going to be a <span style="text-decoration:underline;">flour mill</span> (13A) raised_


_which belonged to a man by the name of <span style="text-decoration:underline;">Reynolds</span> (13A). Captain Black and the_


_Indian agent owned the ground for the millsite. So the Indian agent was_


_sent out to invite the men to the raising of the mill. They learned that_


_I was there so they told him not to come back without me, After he found_


_me and I promised to come he said they had a certain duty for me to pe -_


_form. I asked him what it was and he said it was <span style="text-decoration:underline;">to keep Reynolds in a</span>_


_<span style="text-decoration:underline;">good humor</span> (13A). The men worked out in the hot sun where it was almost un-_


_bearable to be and Mr. Reynolds’ head got so hot that it kept him in a_


_bad humor. He used such abusive language that several of the men quit_


_work and left. So I got the rest of them in a cluster and told them not_


_to get angry at whatever he might say. I soon discovered that he was_


_completely overcome with the heat. So I told him to tell me which way_


_the timbers went and he did, so we soon had the mill up. Although we had_


_no sawed timbers to put in, all being hewed, even the braces and studding,_


_it twisted around so in the hot sun that it’ was hard to get together, but_


_finally it came out all right._


_After that we visited Whitman's Station, expecting to find the handsomest_


_place in the valley. Although a pretty fair place, the site selected for_


_it was between two hills that came near together right in the Indians’ pass_

way. There was two small pair of millstones dressed out of the rock found

_there to put up a small mill where the Indians could get their corn ground._


_Then we returned to Portland and I bought a <span style="text-decoration:underline;">sawmill at Vancouver</span> (14), and ex-_


_pected to get the government trade. I kept it for a while but as I charged_


_them the same as I did everybody else I lost their trade. I built a <span style="text-decoration:underline;">grist</span>_


_<span style="text-decoration:underline;">mill to it</span> (15), and it afterwards <span style="text-decoration:underline;">burnt</span> (15) without any insurance. I then built_


_a <span style="text-decoration:underline;">grist and sawmill where the Hudson Bay Co. had theirs</span> (16). After running_


_that quite a while (16A), it became flooded with high water and several of us_


_took the notion to go to boating._


_We had a meeting. Myself, Farnsworth and J. D. Biles were elected direct-_


_ors. It was almost all thrown onto my shoulders. So I went out to <span style="text-decoration:underline;">Salmon</span>_


_<span style="text-decoration:underline;">Creek and rented a mill</span> (17). I took some men out to dig up the trees with long_


_roots on one side for the bottom timbers of the boat. I would go out one_


_morning and saw till the next evening and bring a load in with me. Farns-_


_worth was a good builder and he wanted to build the boat. Then I had to_


_take charge of the planking under his directions. The O.R.&N. Co. made fun_


_of us, they having two large boats brought here from the East. I made a_


_contract to have the machinery made in Portland. We sent to California for_


_the silders and a few other things, and they were sent up to be finished_


_here. The boiler was too light and before we got through it broke up._


_Then I hired a young man by the day whose name was Dehuff, to finish up the_


_machinery. I got the shop for him to work in, and when it was finished we_


_started to put it in the boat._


_About that time there was an organization of the Peoples' Transportation_


_Co. and we gave them the job of raising the machinery. When it was com-_


_pleted we had the fastest boat on the river. Her name was the <span style="text-decoration:underline;">E. D. BAKER</span> (18)._


_Then I commenced getting out timbers for another boat above the Cascades (18A)._


_I hauled the timber to my own mill and sawed it and sent it above the Cas-_


_cades. We finished that boat and called her the IRIS. We also had an-_


_other boat above Deschutes River. At last a dispute arose between the two_


_companies, to see which would have the route on the Columbia. We commenced_


_cutting rates, till we carried passengers from Portland to The Dalles for_


_50¢. Both companies run in debt until Mr. Ladd made a proposition to com-_


_promise. We gave them two steamboats and freight boats on the Columbia,_


_keeping the E.D. BAKER, and they gave us 7 steamboats and freight boats on_


_the Willamette and $10,000 in cash for us to get off of the Columbia and_


_they got off of the Willamette._


_Then we went to work and put in a <span style="text-decoration:underline;">breakwater at Oregon City</span> (19) 1½  mi. in_


_length and built a <span style="text-decoration:underline;">basin at the falls </span>to run into. Then we built a <span style="text-decoration:underline;">shoot</span>_


_<span style="text-decoration:underline;">to get the freight down to the boats</span> below. I stayed in that business_


_several years and sold out. After I sold out, stock commenced running down_


_till it got to about 30¢ on the dollar, and then a man by the name of Holla-_


_day bought out the whole thing,_


_I then came to Portland and went into the furniture business. I built me a_


_large store (18B) and had it all quite nicely furnished and the big fire of 1873_


_burnt it up and nearly everything in it, without any insurance. Then I had_


_to recruit a few years before I could do much more. A few years after that_


_I put up some <span style="text-decoration:underline;">brick buildings </span>(20) and also some <span style="text-decoration:underline;">wooden ones</span>, which I have yet_


_except what I have given to the heirs._


_The year of 1899, Nov. 29, about four months ago, I commenced to build a_


_<span style="text-decoration:underline;">new residence</span> (21), and expect to put quite a few men to work making more im-_


_provements in a few days. I have been sought after for some time <span style="text-decoration:underline;">forging</span>_


_<span style="text-decoration:underline;">wills and deeds</span> (22) for a large amount of my property. I have stood them off_

There is another interview in the Morning Oregonian of December 4, 1900, under the inviting title “Mr. Love’s Stories”.

_When Mr. Love started to farm on Columbia Slough Indian dances were frequent. Portland was undreamt of._

I somehow doubt the indian dances part. The interview goes on about how Lewis Love got into logging.

_It was costing me a great deal to burn up my timber while clearing. I decided to try rafting to Portland. I put about 20,000 logs in the stream during high water in the Winter of 1852. I then built upright posts and yards on one end of the raft and sailed to Portland. A good strong breeze carried us up the river. We made the trip in about four hours. Another time I came near being caught in a pinch on a contract to deliver logs to the mills in Portland. The water dropped down and a strong current was running in the river. We had to work our way up from the head of the Sauvies Island Slough by an improvised capstan, or windlass, the end of the rope being attached to any convenient object along the shore while the windlass was worked taking up the coil of rope. Lewis Love had a large and complicated family. His children were in the habit of marrying multiple times, and having many children in each marriage. 4.7 The Will

On January 5, 1899 Lewis Love made his final will and testament, which I will (reluctantly) retype in full. It has a useful list of the real property owned and it lists all the heirs. It shows how careful Lewis Love was to make sure this document was known to be his real and only will. As the end of the Manuscript indicates, he had to deal with forged wills and with challenges of his wife’s will after she died. The will also indicates that in all the years he never sold any real estate, he only bought more. The parcels in sections 10, 11, 14, and 16 were 640 acres at the time of the donation land claim, and they were 757 acres at the time of the testament, mostly by extending the original DLC to the west and to the north over the Slough, by buying parts of the Switzler DLC up to Force’s Lake.

Remember that Lewis Love died in 1903. It must have been very frustrating for the heirs that they had to wait for distribution until 1907. The will, and the codicil that follows the will, have been taken from the Love v. Walker et al. lawsuit in the Oregon Supreme Court (Pacific Reporter, 1911).

The codicil does two things. It protects the estate against the scheming husbands and wives of Mary, Fred, and Green and makes sure the property only travels in a straight genealogical line, without reverting to inlaws and other deplorables. In the second place it singles out Green C. Love, who was married in 1876 and had five children. In 1896, however, both his wife and all his children had died. He remarried in 1901 with a bride he had found through a personal ad, which was a method and a resulting bride the Captain did not approve of. So again the special provision had be added to the codicil to make sure the property did not go to outsiders. As we shall see this codicil resulted in lawsuits and court proceedings that lasted more than ten years. More about this later in this chapter.

Even earlier, in 1860 when he was still in his early twenties, William Love acquired a parcel of land of more than 150 acres. I do not know where he got the more than one thousand dollars to pay for it, but let’s review the parcel we are talking about.

On March 10, 1866 the United States registered a military warrant for 160 acres to Charles Vincent, for his service in Private Company H, 2nd Regiment of U.S. Dragoons, in some so-called Apache Indian Disturbance. In terms of modern streets, the land is bounded by Kilpatrick to the north, Lombard to the south, the I-5 to the east, and (approximately) Delaware to the west. https://drive.google.com/open?id=1d0DwA318z6u_lHuJCiMru_CyE84ScvE-

The dates on the deed are interesting. The Military Donation act was passed in 1855, and presumably Charles Vincent claimed his donation shortly after that. Then Robert Maxey sold to William Love in 1860, even though the official warrant was not issued until 1866. If we go over the various homesteads and donation land grants mentioned in this book we see that many of the warrants and patents were not registered until 1866. This delay is not unusual, as Vaughan (2014, p. 45) explains.

_The GLO district land offices were required to certify entitlement to a land patent by issuance of a final certificate upon satisfaction of all statutory requirements by the entryman or purchaser._

And the year 1866 is of special significance. Again from Vaughan (2014, p. 45)

_The issuance of land patents was greatly curtailed during the turbulent Civil War years of 1861 to 1865 – delaying most donation land claim patents until after the war._

In the case of the Maxey-Love transaction there was even more delay. The deed was signed in 1860 in the County of Clark, Territory of Washington, in the presence of William Kelly, Clerk of the District Court. Mr. Kelly basically notarized the signing of the deed, using an oath by a Mr. John Hexler that the person who appeared before him was indeed Robert Maxey. This may not have been enough to actually file the deed with Multnomah County, and in 1877 Roger Greene, associate justice of the Washington Supreme Court, testified that William Kelly was indeed a clerk at that time, and that the laws of Washington authorized him to notarize the deed. In addition, in 1882, a Mr. McConnell, the current clerk and record keeper for the district court, testified that William Kelly was who he said he was, and that his signature was indeed his true signature. So this was 22 years after the original signing. Moreover, as we shall see, it was three years after the death of William Love.

Over the years, William Love became a thriving and well-respected citizen. In the 1870 census the family lived in the Willamette Precinct with six children and two laborers. In 1875 William was appointed Supervisor of Multnomah County road district 3. In 1876 he was appointed Judge of Elections in the Willamette District. He also served on the school board for the district. But all was not well. Below are two articles from the Morning Oregonian, the first from February 12, 1879 and the second from the next day, and one from the Willamette Farmer of February 14. William Love committed suicide by drowning himself in the Columbia Slough. “… he had rid himself of his bodily ills by casting himself into the river.”_ _There is obviously no clear diagnosis of his ailment in these articles from 1879, but the officiating clergyman mentioned that “a protracted illness of nearly two years, accompanied by occasional paroxysms of internal distress, had so far unbalanced his mind as to constitute him periodically insane.”
