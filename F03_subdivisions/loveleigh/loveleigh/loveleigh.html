Subdivisions: Loveleigh

Jan de Leeuw

Version 03-26-2018

Oh, the beauty of the lots,

Sightly lots, and pretty spots,

Give me “Loveleigh” for a home,

Build a cottage, wood or stone,

Then no more I’ll stray or roam

From these lovely beauty spots

“Loveleigh”, of all places wide,

There I’ll take my pretty bride.

F. MacNeill, 726 Patton Road,

02/07/1909

Let’s start with some colorful history. Captain Lewis Love homesteaded a good portion of what is now mostly NE Portland. He came to Oregon in 1849 with his wife and 7 children and established a Donation Land Claim of 640 acres (one square mile). He was a farmer (on the Columbia Slough), grocery store owner, and Justice of the Peace. He operated a ferry service across the Columbia Slough, ran grist and saw mills, a steamboat business, and a hotel. He died a millionaire in 1903 and is buried in the Historic Columbia Cemetery in North Portland (north side of Columbia Blvd, immediately east of the freeway), which actually used to be called Love's Cemetery. After his death his homestead was sold, in various pieces, to developers, after passing through the hands of his offspring.

Elias Brong was active in Portland real estate since 1889, mostly in converting old homesteads into platted city additions. He bought about 50 acres from the Love heirs. In the Morning Oregonian of March 22, 1908, we see the recorded deed of the sale in the real estate section.

We include a picture of the Father of Loveleigh (born in Ohio 1860 - died in Portland 1920).

In the map below modern Piedmont is outlined in red, the Lewis Love Donation Land Claim is outlined in blue, and the Loveleigh subdivision is in solid green. The borders of  Loveleigh are Vancouver in the west (west of Vancouver it is Lovewood - added 1928), Bryant in the south,  Rodney in the east (east of Rodney it is Love's Addition - added 1918), and Winchell (i.e. the railroad tracks) in the north.

The name Loveleigh came from a contest, in which winner Orena F. Rater won a $10 prize for coming up with the name. Brong-Steele did a lot of creative advertising, with promises, prizes, and calculations.  At the end of this paper we have included some of their ads from the Daily Oregonian between 1908 and 1911. Some of these ads mention the Swift Company -- in 1906 Swift bought the local Union Meat Company and started a large meat packing plant on the Columbia River, south of Hayden Island, and platted Kenton as its company town. The ads also mention the El Tovar “sister addition” of Loveleigh in Woodlawn, which has its north-east corner at MLK and Lombard.

Here is an overview picture of the subdivision, looking north,  from a 1908 Brong-Steele ad. Bryant is at the south side, the two large roads going north are Vancouver and Union (Martin Luther King), the smaller roads going north are Williams and Rodney.
