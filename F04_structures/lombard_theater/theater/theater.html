Structures: Lombard Theater

Jan de Leeuw

Version 07-01-2018

Intersection at N Lombard St. and N Albina Ave., circa 1954 : A2005-005.1198.2

Here is a photo, taken around 1954, from the City Archives of the intersection of N Lombard Street and N Albina Avenue, looking west down Lombard toward Interstate. The I-5 isn’t there yet. If we zoom in on the picture we see on the southwest corner Allen’s Columbia Pharmacy, Walsh’s bakery, the Hot-N-Tot Tavern, Buzzy’s, a cycle shop, and the Lombard Theater. In the distance we see the Fred Meyer store that opened in 1953. On the north side we see a gas station, with gas for between 25 and 30 cents a gallon. It seems like an Arco station, and a sign seems to say “Gas Free Every Monday”. Further west on the north side of Lombard we see a cafe and what looks like a record store, with maybe a sign advertising Wanda Jackson (who started her career around 1955 and had her first top 40 hit in 1960). Beyond that  a tire store and maybe an antique mall.

In order to zoom in, download the photo and look at it in a suitable viewer application -- don’t zoom using your web browser, because that will not give you enough pixels. You should be able to see that the Lombard Theater is showing Jane Wyman in “Magnificent Obsession”, a glorious 1954 Douglas Sirk movie, and John Wayne in “Tall in the Saddle”, a fairly standard 1944 western. I am hoping some of our long-term residents can fill me in about these other businesses. For now, I am just looking at the Lombard Theater.

It was first mentioned in the Oregonian in November 1943, as the New Lombard Theater. So maybe it was new at that time.

The next mention is in November 1953, at the opening of the new Fred Meyer Shopping Center on Interstate and Lombard. Free theater parties for children at the Lombard Theater on behalf of Fred Meyer. No tickets needed. FREE comic books, gum, candy, balloons.

We have already seen that in the fifties and sixties, the Lombard Theater was a fairly regular movie theater, with relative standard and recent Hollywood fare. In April, 1965, for example, they were showing Mutiny on the Bounty (1962) and Rio Conchos (1964).

In the early seventies the Lombard Theater was actually showing interesting films. They played Woodstock (1970), with live music. They showed the Joan Baez/David Harris documentary Carry it On (1970). IMDB says

CARRY IT ON features folksinger Joan Baez and husband David Harris, a leader of the draft resistance movement, during the powerful political currents of the late 1960s. The film follows Baez on a musical tour of protest, including Woodstock, features live recordings of 13 major songs, and gives a behind-the-scenes view of Baez.

In the same year there was a Kurosawa film festival, featuring Rashomon and Throne of Blood. A French film festival, presented by Jacques Demy himself. But then, something happened.

In 1972 the world saw the premiere of Deep Throat and Behind the Green Door. Hardcore porn came to theaters where it had not been before. The Oregonian of April 20, 1972 says it all.

There is no more mention of the Lombard theater after 1974. It still mentioned in some real estate advertisements for apartments on Russell Street, one block north of the Lombard Theater.

As this (undated) photo by Gary Graver shows, the theater was not doing well.

And as the current Google street maps shows, the building is sort of restored, but it has completely lost its former role as a rare cultural center of the Piedmont Neighborhood.
