History of the Piedmont Neighborhood 1850-1920

3 Introduction

Writing a chapter about Lewis Love is somewhat daunting. He had a long, rich, and active life.

If I had enough time and energy left I could probably write a 250-page book about Lewis Love and his immediate family, but I have many more Piedmont history chapters to write. I am just mentioning the potential Lewis Love book to show how much happened during his life, and how much material there still is.

Even for my more limited project there is much more documentation I have to go through than for Ulery, Fenstermacher, Smith, and Howe combined. Love had many children, who largely remained in the Portland area, and generated many newspaper items of their own. He had much more land in his donation land claim than the others, because he was married and arrived in Oregon early. He also lived longer, and he was involved in multiple minor and major lawsuits, which also generated a lot of coverage. So this promises to be a rather long chapter, with much biographical detail.

There will be separate sections on the members of his immediate family, his wife and children.

Because this book concentrates on real property transactions, we only discuss the children, because after the death of Lewis Love they almost immediately got rid of his land. The grandchildren were no longer involved in selling tracts and subdividing neighborhoods. Unfortunately, but not surprisingly, not much is known about his wife and the daughters. Their assigned role in life was to marry young, get a lot of children, and “keep house”. There are some minor exceptions, but generally it’s patriarchy throughout.

One more introductory remark. The modern Piedmont neighborhood tends to be identified with the original Piedmont subdivision of 1888. For example, the motto of the Piedmont Neighborhood Association is taken from the brochures of the Investment Company, the logo is either the bandstand in Peninsula Park, right next to original Piedmont, or the water tower that the Investment Company built for the Piedmont subdivision. But the part of the Piedmont subdivision that is in the Piedmont neighborhood is only about ten to fifteen percent of the neighborhood area, while the Lewis Love DLC is at least half of the Piedmont neighborhood. A pretty good case could be made for changing the name of the Piedmont neighborhood to the Love neighborhood, or simply to Lewis Love.

Of course it is true that the southern part of the neighborhood, below Bryant, is among the oldest parts and was certainly initially the most affluent. But one of the main reasons for this is that Lewis Love did not sell or develop any of his acreage, and that development north of Bryant only started more than twenty years after Piedmont was platted and developed. In those twenty years North Portland built the Swift meat packing plant, and many other industries, and the people working in those plants needed housing. That created demand for a different type of housing than the homes for comparatively well-off people fleeing the rowdy and immoral West Side.

To some extent this chapter tries to remedy an unfortunate situation. The Sunday Oregonian of January 7, 2008 had a nice article about Lewis Love in John Terry’s Oregon’s Trails series with the title: _Pioneer’s name all but forgotten in public domain. _Here it is:

Although the piece is useful, it has several errors. As we have seen, the southern boundary of Love’s property in North/Northeast Portland was not Lombard but Bryant. Although there may not be a great deal of Love memorabilia left standing downtown, the names of the Piedmont subdivisions certainly have a lot of Love in them. I guess John Terry, together with the rest of the Oregonian, tends to pay more attention to the other side of the Willamette. This also explains why Lewis Love was “all but forgotten”.

Portland history, like most local history, tends to concentrate on the people who started living in the town center, were active in public life, flaunted their riches, donated stuff, and advertised their name. Lewis Love amassed his fortune in a rather unspectacular and fairly modest way. Although he was active on the west side, and actually lived on First Street and Front Street for a long time, his most important accomplishments were in Washington Territory, on the Columbia River, and in North and Northeast Portland. And events in those areas were inherently less visible than those on the west side. His children stayed on the east side and platted and developed his huge land holdings there. Although it is true that Lewis Love does not feature prominently in Portland history books such as the ones by McCall, Abbott, and Lansing, his name does live on in the names of the subdivisions Loveleigh, Lovewood, Love’s Addition, and the Green C. Love Addition.

I hope this chapter shows how Captain Love’s trajectory, starting poor and becoming rich, was ultimately based on huge swatches of stolen lands given away by the US government. The purpose of this generosity was to attract settlers from the East to replace Native Americans and to finalize discovery and manifest destiny claims. Within this framework, which is of course basically corrupt, Lewis Love was a remarkable man. He saw and took opportunities that were offered to white people at the time, creating and abandoning businesses when appropriate, never being discouraged by disaster, using and misusing the law, and having a truly incredible amount of physical and mental power and energy. And, throughout, he saw and emphasized the value of land. Holding on for more than 50 years to his 700 acres in North and Northeast Portland, and not selling any of it during his lifetime, was definitely a prudent decision. And above all it was very family-friendly, because ultimately it greatly benefited all his children and grandchildren.
