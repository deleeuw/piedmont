The Land

The Piedmont subdivision is 160 acres of land, bounded by (what is now) Rosa Parks Way, Martin Luther King Jr Boulevard, Killingsworth Street, and Commercial/Kerby Avenue. Although the neighborhood and the subdivision have the same name, only about 10% of the current Piedmont neighborhood is in the Piedmont subdivision. It is, however, the oldest developed part. In this chapter I write the history of the Piedmont subdivision, even though I will be talking about only a small part of the Piedmont neighborhood.

I have reviewed the history of the 160 acres that make up the subdivision in the chapter on the 1866 Henry Walsh military donation claim. George and Elizabeth Smith obtained his land for an undisclosed amount and added 80 acres of Evander Howe’s homestead to the west. They registered the deeds in 1870 and sold the 240 acres to A.P. Dennison and H.F. Bloch for $ 2,400 in the same year. In 1872 Bloch and Dennison sold the eastern 160 acres, which would become the Piedmont subdivision, to P.J. Martin and D.F. Harrington for $ 5,000. The land stayed in the hands of the Martin clan until 1888, although with much back and forth and legal wrangling, all documented in the homestead chapter. In 1888 D.V.B. Henarie, of the Martin Company, sold to the parcel Charles A. Neal for $ 24,000, who immediately sold it to the Investment Company for the same $ 24,000. And that was the last sale before a plat was filed.

The Plat

The plat map for the Town of Piedmont was filed on October 16th, 1889,  by the Investment Company. The Investment  Company incorporated on October 14, 1887, with the explicit purpose of investing in real estate, mainly in what is now Northeast Portland. Their first, and largest, project was the laying out and platting of the Piedmont subdivision, which was half a mile long and half a mile wide for a total of 160 acres, and over 600 city lots. A later, much smaller, project was the Beverly subdivision, consisting of about 65 lots immediately north of Piedmont.

Oregonian, 10-15-1887

The filing document describing the plat is three pages, one with a map, and two with text. I only include the map in this chapter, with a link to the full document. I transcribe the text to save readers the trouble of deciphering 19th century longhand.

Some names on the map stand out. What is now Killingsworth Street used to be State Street. What is now Martin Luther King Jr Boulevard used to be Fourth Street. Ainsworth Street used to be Center Street. There is no street to the north of the plat, because Portland Boulevard, later Rosa Parks Way, did not yet exist. There is also nothing to the west, because there is no Kerby Avenue yet. Here is a cutout of 1889 Multnomah County to show the general area at the time. Also note the Portland and Vancouver Railroad on Fourth Street, then making a 45 degree turn east at the northeast corner of Piedmont to avoid going over Lewis Love’s land and in the process tilting some streets in Woodlawn off the regular grid.

The text follows. Note that it describes all streets and alleys in detail, because they will be dedicated to the public domain, so it is best to read the text while looking at the map. It is long, and mostly boring, but it also has some interesting and curious parts.

Know all men by these presents that the Investment Company, a corporation created under the general incorporation laws of the State of Oregon, having its principal place of business at Portland, Oregon do hereby layout and dedicate the Southeast Quarter of Section Fifteen in Township One North Range One East in Multnomah County, State of Oregon, as a Town, the same to be known and designated as Piedmont, and, as marked and designated upon the accompanying Map and Plat of said Town of Piedmont, herewith filed and recorded in the Recorder’s Office of said County of Multnomah State of Oregon. The said Plat is constructed upon the scale of 200 feet to the inch and the dimensions of Lots and Blocks and the width and location of streets and alleys are thereupon indicated. All the following named streets and intermediate alleys running North and South extend from the South line of said tract to within fifty feet of the North line of the same, to wit, Fourth Street, A Street, C Street, D Street, E Street and F Street. Vancouver Avenue extends through the Tract. The following named street running north and South extend together with the two alleys lying next to the East of them from Gilbert Avenue on the South to a point fifty feet South of the North line of the Tract, to wit H Street and I Street, excepting that the alley lying to the East of H Street extends Northward only to Myrtle Street. The following named streets running East and West extend from the East line of the Tract to a point 15 feet from the West line of the same, to wit: Myrtle Street, Elm Street, and Gilbert Avenue. Center Street extends from the East line of the Tract to the West line of the Alley 185 feet West of Vancouver Avenue, Pearl Street extends from the East line of the Tract to Vancouver Avenue. Gilbert Avenue and Vancouver Avenue are each eighty feet wide. All other streets, except those hereinafter named, are sixty feet wide. State Street is thirty feet wide. All alleys are fifteen feet wide. That portion of Fourth Street lying West of the center of the Portland and Vancouver Railway track for a width of thirty feet is dedicated to general and Public use, the remainder of the Street, to wit that portion lying West of a line parallel with the West line of said Street and thirty feet distant therefrom is dedicated to the use of the adjoining property owners, and may be controlled, used, or improved in such manner as two thirds of the owners of the lots fronting directly upon said Fourth Street may elect. This dedication is made subject to the following restrictions and reservations, to wit: All water, gas, and sewer pipes, and all electric, telegraph, and telephone wires are hereafter and forever excluded from all Streets and are confined to the alleys, excepting that water, gas, and sewer pipes may be laid where necessary upon Centre and Myrtle Streets and upon the West side of F Street and excepting further that pipes supplying Fire cisterns may be located in Streets where absolutely necessary so to be laid, and that electric wires for street lighting may be run in shortest direction from nearest alley to the corners where such lights are to be placed; said wires to be so placed as to cause the least possible obstruction or inconvenience and excepting further that electric wires for telephone or lighting purposes of F Street may be run on the West side of such Street. Alleys may be used for all of the above named purposes and the various pipes and wires as specified may cross the Streets abutting on said Alleys where necessary and not otherwise except as hereinbefore designated. Vehicles of every character propelled by steam are forever excluded from said Streets and Alleys. Cable, electric or horse cars may be used on any of said Streets by consent of two thirds of the property owners, owning property within one hundred feet of the Street so proposed to be used. This Dedication and all conveyances to be made of land in said Town shall be subject to the restrictions and covenants in said conveyances to be said forth, providing by the terms thereof that if at any time the purchasers or owners of any property in said Town, or those holding or claiming the same under them, shall, with the knowledge or consent of the owner of such premises use, or cause to be used, or shall allow or authorize in any manner, directly or indirectly, said premises or any part thereof, or the Street or Alley in front or along said premises for the purposes of manufacturing or vending intoxicating liquors for drinking purposes, whether said manufacturing or vending shall be directly or under some evasive guise, thereupon the title so granted shall revert to and be vested in the grantors, the owners so dedicating the said Town, their successors or assignees, and the said corporation, its successors or assignees shall be entitled to the immediate possession thereof, provided that any bona fide mortgagee of said premises in case the said covenant be broken shall have the option to at once claim and enforce said reversion for himself and for his own use and benefit subject however to the foregoing covenant running against any further violation thereof, otherwise said covenant shall have the same force and effect as if the said proviso were not herein contained. This Dedication and all conveyances to be made of the properties as platted shall be made subject to the further provision that whenever the owners of two thirds of the properties in said Town situated within One Hundred feet of any Street shall desire to improve such Street, and shall agree upon the time and method of such improvement, the owner of each parcel so situated within such limits, shall upon due notice pay his proportionate share of the expense of such improvement, said expense to be assessed by said property owners apportioned in the same manner as like expense of improvement of streets is apportioned in the City of Albina. In Witness Whereof the said Investment Company had by virtue of a Resolution of its Board of Directors duly adopted, caused these presents to be signed by E. Quackenbush its President and its Corporate Seal to be hereto affixed this the fifteenth day of October 1889.

Apart from the tedious descriptions of streets and alleys, there are a few remarkable parts of the document. The first is that the plat makes room for a system of wide alleys between any two streets. Those alleys, moreover, have all the utility pipes and wires in them, so that the streets remain relatively pristine. The second interesting restriction is that steam vehicles are prohibited, and other vehicles are only allowed if two-thirds of the homeowners agree they can be there. If this provision were still in place it would solve a lot of the parking problems. Also note that the homeowners on a street or in a subdivision had some actual power over how their neighborhood looked. And finally, most spectacularly, there was a prohibition against manufacturing or selling alcoholic beverages throughout Piedmont, on penalty of losing one’s property to the Investment Company or to another mortgagee. These clauses, which are extensive and merciless, are no doubt personally put in by Edward Quackenbush, who was a principled teetotaler, a co-founder of the Portland YMCA, and a charter member of the Oregon Anti-Saloon League. Although I will write a separate chapter on Quackenbush at some point in time, I think no chapter on Piedmont is complete without his portrait.

Again it shows the efforts of Quackenbush to keep alcoholic beverages, saloons, and cheap housing out of Piedmont, to prevent it from becoming like the dens of iniquity and sin on the other side of the Willamette. And, actually, to keep Piedmont “a place for homes”, by basically banning all commercial development.

Streets

In 1891 the cities of Portland, Albina, and East Portland consolidated to the new city of Portland. As a consequence the street names in the new city were a mess. There were alphabet names (A Street, B Street, and so on) and numerical names (First Street, and so on) in all three cities. At the old city boundaries streets suddenly changed their names. Thus there was “An Ordinance Providing for the Change of the Names of Certain Streets in the City of Portland” (Ordinance 7263 of January 12, 1892). The name of the ordinance is somewhat modest, but in fact hundreds of streets and street segments changed their names. Here is a link to the full text of the ordinance.

https://drive.google.com/open?id=0B94Urj3OjM7BdXJlNDBOd2VjUTA

Those are still not the final versions of the names. Hendricks Ave became Rodney Avenue on January 21, 1904, Colfax became Holman on August 23, 1907, and Pearl became Jessup on August 23, 1907. A further batch of changes came with ordinance 61325, passed September 2, 1931. All north-south streets such as Moore and Commercial became “Avenues”, all east-west streets such as Ainsworth and Killingsworth became “Streets”. Moreover every street or street section east of Williams Avenue got Northeast (NE) in front of their name, while west of Williams every street got North (N). And, much later, on April 20, 1989, Union Avenue became Martin Luther King Jr Boulevard, and on November 24, 2006 Portland Boulevard became Rosa Parks Way. The only name surviving from the original plat map is Vancouver Avenue, which was of course part of the old county road connecting Portland and Vancouver.

Amenities

From the very beginning Piedmont was advertised as a nice and quiet neighborhood, far from the riffraff that hung out on the other side of the Willamette River. We have seen the strict inhibition of manufacturing and sales of intoxicating beverages, which clearly was intended to set the neighborhood apart from downtown Portland, which was riddled with saloons and boarding houses of doubtful repute. But there were some innovations that improved the neighborhood, and that are still of importance some 130 years later. The alleys, and the laying the pipes and wires in the alleys, are a prime example. More than 1000 boxed shade trees, six to nine feet high, were planted. There was no obligation to build a house on the lot, but if you did there was a restriction on the price of homes, which had to cost at least $ 1,500 to $ 3,000, depending on location, in order to ensure that no cheap homes were built. Both lots and homes were priced on a sliding scale, with the homes on the east side (near the railway and near Woodlawn) more expensive, and those of the west side (near a large undeveloped and overgrown tract) less expensive. This was known at the time as a “surroundings guaranteed” strategy.

We include some clips from the Oregonian of 1889-1890, some of which are straightforward advertisements, some of which look like articles, but could equally well serve as advertisements. They prove that Quackenbush was well connected, and used his connections for some old-fashioned boosterism.

Water Works

Of course providing water, sewers, and electricity is one of the important phases for new developments, certainly in previously undeveloped areas. Water is especially important, because both quality and quantity need to be satisfactory, and the water has to come at a reasonable price. Around 1890, at the same time that Piedmont was platted, the City of Portland began developing the Bull Run river as its primary source of drinking water (as it still is). Bull Run water did not become available, however, until 1895, too late for Piedmont. So a separate Piedmont Water Works company was incorporated, a well was dug, and a pumphouse added to pump the water to a water tower.

Oregonian April 19, 1890

In the Oregonian of January 26, 1892 we see when the Piedmont Water Works came on line.

A force of men is at work getting the Piedmont Water Works in shape for operation.  The machinery and pumps are nearly ready, and service pipes are being extended to the residences.

A cut out from the 1901 Sanford map shows that the water works were located at the end of Williams Avenue, on the corner of the as yet non-existing Portland Boulevard. The water tower was sometimes used as an icon for the Piedmont Subdivision, for example in the header of the Piedmont Neighborhood Association website.

The photo of the water tower from the OHS archives shows its size, why it was a landmark,  and why the lower and upper parts were used for various events. The Oregonian of September 18, 1891, says

… the Piedmont Water Tower is a decidedly architectural structure, 110 feet high, surmounted by an observatory. The third floor, 50 feet by 60, is set apart for an assembly hall. The well is lined with brick, laid in cement, and goes to the river level, 200 feet, so of course the water is clear, cool, and abundant.

The caption under the OHS photo of the tower is not entirely correct. As we shall see, the tower was destroyed in 1914, not 1917, and arson was suspected, not proved.

It is of some interest to look into why someone would want to set this iconic architectural  structure on fire. I think that the trouble started when the Investment Company, in the best tradition of boosters and developers then and now, could not deliver what it promised. In 1893 everything was still going strong. The Oregonian of May 24 says:

Arrangements have been made by which Piedmont and Woodlawn will have reasonable fire protection. At Piedmont a volunteer company has been organized with 39 members. Frank Motter was elected president, Mr. Bard vice-president and Mr. Paget secretary. For the present the company has quarters in the water tower hall. The company will be supplied with 1000 feet of hose with reel, and seven fire hydrants are to be attached to the mains of the Piedmont waterworks. All the pipe at Piedmont is of iron, and the pressure is very good.

After 1895, however, the competitive pressure of city-provided Bull Run water rapidly become too much to handle. The Investment Company was eager to sell.

Oregonian, January 23, 1902

One reason for the desired switch was explained in a long article in the Oregonian of August 5, 1902.

The breakdown of the Piedmont water plant 10 days ago, causing a shortage of water to over 130 families, has caused a general clamour for city water. A petition for water was turned down some time ago, for the reason there is no improved street in the district. The cause of the accident to the water plant was the giving way of the pump. Water was supplied by means of a water cart, which went from house to house. The cost to the consumers per faucet is just double what is charged for Bull Run water.

The citizens of Piedmont and Woodlawn wanted Bull Run water, because it was less expensive and more reliable. Getting it, however, was dependent on the success of another initiative. First Union Avenue had to be further improved from Alberta to at least Dekum, so that a water main could be put in that connected to the city’s system at Mount Tabor. There were other reasons to improve Union Avenue, of course, but the possibility of getting Bull Run water was one of them.

The Oregonian of November 9, 1905 reports on a meeting of the Water Board:

It was asserted by Mr. Kellahar afterwards, that Ladd and Killingsworth own the Piedmont water plant that supplies Piedmont and Woodlawn with water, and that some of the consumers are charged as much as $3 a month for household use, and $5 a month for a lawn, while none are charged less than $2, and that the water supply is uncertain. In consequence the people have joined in the general cry for relief.

After some haggling the City of Portland bought the Piedmont Water Company for $ 20,000 on April 23, 1907. The deed was recorded in July and Bull Run water came to Piedmont around the same time, because there now indeed was a large main along Union Avenue. See the articles from the 1907 Oregonian.

The remaining problem was the water tower, no longer in use, and now owned by the City. The good citizens of Piedmont soon wanted to get rid of the tower, because it was turning into “a rendezvous for tramps”. That, then as now, is sure to bring out waves of NIMBYism.

Oregonian, February 26, 1914

A week or so after the petition to the City Council there was no longer any need for a decision by the austere body. The tower caught fire. “Many theories as to the origin of the fire were considered by firemen in attendance”.

Oregonian, March 8, 1914

It seems the tower refused to go down without putting up a fight. As reported in the Oregon Daily Journal of March 7, 1914 it made a valiant attempt to take at least some of the good citizens of Piedmont with it into oblivion. It failed, and merely made some women scream.

 

Oregon Daily Journal, March 07, 1914

Evergreen Park

We have already seen that the home owners in Piedmont by deed and contract did have some power to influence the various decisions the Investment Company made. The change to Bull Run water is one example, the story of Evergreen Park is a second one. It is told in two other chapters of this book, the one on Evergreen Park and the one on Liverpool Liz.

The Paving Controversy

As a final example of the influence of the homeowners on what happened in Piedmont I briefly discuss street paving. As we have seen in the chapter on the Peninsula Boulevard System hard surfacing the streets on the East Side was taking off around 1910. In Ordinance 21836 of August 5, 1910 the City of Portland decided to pave Union Avenue from Alberta Street to Bryant Street with Hassam pavement on a concrete foundation. This decision was taken after close to 20 years agitation by Piedmont and Woodlawn home and business owners, who in the meantime were now agitating for extending and paving Union Avenue from Bryant to the Columbia River. Details are in the Union Avenue chapter.

In 1907 none of the streets in Piedmont were graded or paved, which is why the Water Board refused to take over the water supply. But in November 1908 the City signed a contract with the Barber Asphalt Company to hard surface Cleveland Avenue from Killingsworth to Ainsworth. The Investment Company sued the City (as well as Mayor Lane and City Auditor Barbur) because: “Owning 22 lots there, the company shows that the cost of street improvement will fall heavily on the Investment Company without proportionate benefits”. Allegedly the cost of the paving was $ 10,000, and the City did not engage in a competitive bidding process, and awarded the contract to a company with a de facto monopoly.

Seemingly this was not the only lawsuit. In the Oregonian of February 21, 1909 the Investment Company sued the same parties, contesting a contract for improvements in Piedmont awarded to the Oregon Hassam Paving Company.

It is charged that the City Engineer prepared his specifications in such a manner as to leave no room for competition. For the specifications call for “hard-surface Hassam pavement” and there is but one company in the city which handles that material, other companies being shut out from its use because of existing patents.

A quick perusal of the Oregonian at the time shows that there are quite a few scandals and lawsuits going on related to paving Portland streets. The Piedmont lawsuits, however, put the Investment Company in a conflict with not just the City, but also with Piedmont homeowners. In the Oregonian of March 12, 1909, we read:

George P. Lent, representing the property owners of Piedmont, told the court that the feeling among his constituents is strong against Mr. Quackenbush. He said that many of those who have built homes in Piedmont want street improvements, and that this is continually fought by the Investment Company, as Mr. Lent says the company does not wish to pay for the improvements, although it owns a considerable share of the property in that part of the city, and is disposing of it.

In the Block Book we could see that in 1906 many lots were still owed by the Investment Company. The Sanborn maps show that in 1908-1909, twenty years after the plat was filed, still only about a quarter of the lots was developed. Of course the maps also show the shape and size of the houses and their auxiliary buildings.

In 1924 the Sanborn maps show that now at least half of the lots were developed. Piedmont is covered by twelve different maps, and we will link to them using the same N-W-S-E descriptions we used for the Block Book maps, organizing them in three West-Central-East blocks with four maps each, going from North to South.
